Abstract 11 

Drug discovery remains one of the costliest and most time -intensive endeavors in the 12 

pharmaceutical pipeline, with average development costs exceeding $2.3 billion per drug, 13 

timelines spanning more than a deca de, and attrition rates above 90% in clinical trials. While 14 

computational methods have expanded the searchable chemical space, current pipelines remain 15 

fragmented and largely inaccessible to researchers without deep interdisciplinary expertise. Here 16 

we present F.A.D.E. (Fully Agentic Drug Engine), a multi -agent, open -source platform that 17 

converts natural language queries into potential drug candidates, substantially lowering the 18 

expertise barrier to advanced computational drug discovery. F.A.D.E. employs a three -branch 19 

hierarchical architecture that adapts to the level of available structural data for any protein target, 20 

integrating structure prediction, binding pocket detection, equivariant diffusion -based de novo 21 

ligand generation, and binding affinity estimation into a single automated pipeline. We validate 22 

F.A.D.E. on two structurally distinct targets: the epidermal growth factor receptor kinase domain 23 

(EGFR), a well -established oncology target, and cellular retinol -binding protein 1 (CRBP1), a 24 

lipid -bind ing protein involved in retinoid metabolism. For EGFR, our generated candidates 25 

achieved QED scores of 0.85 compared to 0.46 for the co -crystallised reference ligand, 26 

demonstrating marked improvement in predicted drug -likeness. Results across both targets 27 

confirm that F.A.D.E. can reliably generate chemically tractable, drug -like hit compounds across 28 

diverse protein classes from simple natural language input. 29

1. Introduction 36 

Drug discovery remains prohibitively expensive and time -consuming, often taking over a decade 37 

and billions of dollars to bring a single drug to market (DiMasi et al. 2016; Mullard 2014; Wouters 38 

et al. 2020). While computational approaches have emerged to accelerate this process, they face 39 

critical limitations such as proprietary constraints that lock out broader research communities, 40 

fragmented pipeline s that require specialised expertise across multiple domains, and AI -driven 41 

solutions that often lack the rigorous physics -based validation necessary for reliable predictions. 42 

These challenges create significant barriers to entry and slow innovation in developing life -saving 43 

therapeutics. 44 

There have been notable recent advances in structure -based drug design, including diffusion -based 45 

generative models (Schneuing et al. 2024; Yim et al. 2023 ; Wu et al. 2024), binding affinity 46 

predictors (Passaro et al. 202 5), and protein structure prediction (Jumper et al. 2021; Passaro et al. 47 

2025 ). However, these tools have remained largely fragmented and require considerable technical 48 

expertise, severely limiting the ability of early -stage computational researchers or experimentalists 49 

to move seamlessly from a drug discovery -based query to a viable output. Several recent efforts 50 

have sought to address this fragmentation by building LLM -powered agentic frameworks for 51 

chemical and biological research. ChemCrow (M. Bran et al. 2024) was among the first to 52 

demonstrate that augmenting an LLM with a suite of chemistry -specific tools could enable 53 

autonomous execution of drug discovery tasks. Coscientist (Boiko et al. 2023) proved that LLM 54 

agents can plan and execute chemical experiments with minimal human intervention by 55 

coordinating robotic laboratory platforms. DrugAgent (Liu et al. 2024) introduced a multi -agent 56 

framework specifically targeting the automation of machine learning programming tasks within 57 

drug discovery, achievi ng strong performance in ADMET prediction workflows. AgentD (Ock et 58 

al. 2026) further extended this paradigm to an end -to -end LLM -powered agent capable of data 59 

retrieval, molecular generation, multi -property prediction, and 3D protein –ligand structure 60 

generation from a single interface. Similarly, the Prompt -to -Pill (Vichentijevikj et al. 2025) 61

Despite these advances, existing agentic frameworks have largely focused on property prediction 66

2. Methods 105 

2.1 System Arc hitecture and Agentic Workflow 106 

F.A.D.E. is implemented as a multi -agentic, multi -layered workflow in which dedicated agents 107 

handle discrete stages of the drug discovery pipeline (see Figure 1 ). Users interact with the system 108 

via a natural language interface, with a dedicated agent parsing and processing the input query. 109 

Upon identifying the target of interest, this agent then directs downstream agents responsible for 110 

database retrieval, pocket identification, molecule generation, and property scoring. The m odular 111 

architecture enables individual components to be updated or replaced independently as more 112 

accurate computational tools become available. 113 

114

2.3 Binding Pocket Generation using fpocket 157 

Following the hierarchical database search and structure retrieval (Section 2.2), the next critical 158 

step in the F.A.D.E. workflow is the accurate identification of the binding pockets in the protein 159 

structure. When the workflow follows Branch 1 , the binding site is extracted directly from the 160 

experimentally determined co -crystallised ligand position, bypassing the need for computational 161 

prediction. For cases following Branch 2 or Branch 3 , wherein the holo structure is absent or is 162 

of low resolution, the open -source tool fpocket is used for binding pock et detection. fpocket is a 163 

robust protein cavity detection tool that identifies druggable binding sites on the protein surface 164 

by analysing the geometry of the protein and its physicochemical properties. 165 

166 

The algorithm identifies binding sites by calculating a druggability score for each detected pocket. 167 

This score, ranging from 0 to 1, is estimated based on several key features, including the number 168 

of alpha spheres, density, polarity, mean local hydrophobic density, and the proportion of apolar 169 

alpha sph eres. The pockets that are assigned a high druggability score (typically greater than 0.5) 170 

are considered viable binding domains. These identified coordinates are then passed to the de novo 171 

ligand generation module (Section 2.4) to accurately determine drug -like candidates for the 172 

calculated binding pockets. This ensures that F.A.D.E. can reliably identify potential ligand -173 

binding sites even in the absence of explicit, high -resolution experimental binding information. 174 

175

2.5 Binding Affinity Prediction and Candidate Ranking 193 

Generated candidates are ranked by a combination of QED scores, synthetic accessibility (SA) 194 

score (Ertl and Schuffenhauer 2009 ) and predicted binding affinity values. SA scores reflect the 195 

estimated ease of chemical synthesis, with lower values indicating compounds that are more 196 

readily synthesisable. Binding affinities are estimated using Boltz -2, which provides rapid binding 197 

affinity predictions from protein -ligand complexes without relying on molecular dynamics 198 

simulations or rigorous free energy calculations. This integration of these metrics allows F.A.D.E. 199 

to prioritize candidates that are not only geometrically complementary to the target pocket but also 200 

drug -like, synthesizable, and predicted to be high -affinity binders. 201

2.6 Case Study Targets 203 

F.A.D.E. was validated on two targets. The first, EGFR, is a receptor tyrosine kinase that is well -204 

established in l ung cancer -targeting therapies. Both apo and holo structures were readily extracted 205 

from the RCSB database, making this target well -suited to demonstrate Branch 1 of the workflow 206 

(see section 2.2 in Methods) . The second, CRBP1, is a lipid -binding protein involved in vitamin 207 

A (retinol) transport and metabolism. While both apo and holo conformations were found in the 208 

RCSB database, the holo structures are generally of lower resolution. This target was therefore 209 

selected to demonstrate Branch 2 of the workflo w. 210 

Branch 3 of the F.A.D.E. workflow is designed to perform de novo structure prediction from 211 

sequence via Boltz -2, followed by fpocket -based pocket identification and DiffSBDD -based 212 

ligand generation. In practice, the majority of commonly studied drug targets have at least an apo 213 

structure deposited in the PDB, so Branches 1 and 2 cover most routine use cases. Branch 3 is 214 

intended primarily for novel or poorly characterized targets for which no experimental structure 215 

exists. While the platform's architecture supports this pathway, its full implementation has not yet 216 

been completed, and the two case studies presented here exercise Branches 1 and 2 only. 217 

Completing and validating Branch 3 , including benchmarking the predicted structures against 218 

experimentally determined ground -truth structures, remains a priority for extending F.A.D.E.'s 219 

coverage to this harder regime and is an immediate next step for this work. 220

4. Discuss ion 293 

Our findings demonstrate that F.A.D.E. represents a meaningful step toward the democratization 294 

and large -scale integration of computational drug discovery tools. By combining natural language 295 

interaction with agentic AI, cutting -edge physics -based tools, and binding affinity predictions 296 

within a single automated workflow, the platform removes key barriers that have confined 297 

advanced computational methods to specialised research groups. The results on EGFR and CRBP1 298 

demonstrate that F.A.D.E. can reliably generate drug -like candidates across chemically diverse 299 

targets, providing a foundation for broader therapeutic exploration. 300 

Compared to existing agentic frameworks, F.A.D.E. offers several distinguishing features. Unlike 301 

ChemCrow (M. Bran et al. 2024) and DrugAgent (Liu et al. 2024), which primarily focus on tool 302 

augmentation for property prediction and ADMET workflows, F.A.D.E. integrates structure -based 303 

de novo ligand generation through DiffSBDD, producing entirely novel molecular scaffolds rather 304 

than screening existing compound libraries. AgentD (Ock et al. 2026) shares F.A.D.E.’s end -to -305 

end ambition but relies on a single linear pipeline, whereas F.A.D.E.’s three -branch hierarchical 306 

architecture allows the system to adapt its strategy to the level of available structural data. The 307 

Prompt -to -Pill framework (Vichentijevikj et al. 2025) proposes the broadest scope, spanning the 308 

full preclinical pipeline, but remains largely conceptual; by contrast, F.A.D.E. presents validated 309

generated candidates (0.85 versus 0.46 for the reference co -crystallised ligand) suggests that the 315 

generative pipeline inherently favours drug -like chemical compounds. This is cons istent with the 316 

design of DiffSBDD, which samples from a learned distribution over drug -like chemical space 317 

conditioned on pocket geometry, rather than optimising against a single binding affinity objective. 318 

While the predicted binding affinities of generated compounds did not surpass the reference ligand, 319 

this outcome reflects the intended design of the current pipeline, which prioritises diverse, drug -320 

like scaffold generation over single -property optimization. 321 

322 

The binding affinity results warrant careful interpretation. The weaker predicted affinities of 323 

F.A.D.E. -generated candidates relative to the co -crystallised reference ligands do not necessarily 324 

indicate poor therapeutic potential, since the reference ligands have undergone extensive medicinal 325 

chemistry optimisation, whereas the generated candidates represent initial hit compounds. It is also 326 

important to note that Boltz -2’s affinity estimation is not a simple scoring function applied to a 327 

rigid receptor: the model jointly predicts the three -dimensional arrangement of both the protein 328 

and ligand in the bound state, meaning the receptor conformation in the output may differ from 329 

the input crystal structure as the model predicts conformational adjustments upon ligand binding. 330 

The affinity values are therefore derived from these predicted complexes rather than from rigid 331 

docking. Nonetheless, these predictions have not been systematically benchmarked against 332 

experimental binding data for de novo generated compounds, and the absolute values should be 333 

interpreted with caution. The primary value of these predictions within the current pipeline lies in 334 

their use as a relative ranking metric among generated candidates rather than as absolute measures 335 

of binding strength. Future iterations of F.A.D.E. will incorporate more rigorous affinity estimation 336 

methods, such as molecular dynamics -based free energy perturbation or MM -GBSA rescoring, to 337 

provide higher -confidence affinity predictions and enable direct comparison with experimental 338 

IC50 or Kd val ues. 339

5. Conclusion 353 

We have introduced F.A.D.E., a fully agentic, open -source drug discovery platform that enables 354 

researchers to generate and evaluate small -molecule drug candidates through natural language 355 

queries. By orche strating a hierarchical workflow that adapts to the level of available structural 356 

data, F.A.D.E. demonstrates robust performance across distinct protein targets. 357 

The platform's key achievements include widening the accessibility of computational drug 358 

discovery tools through a conversational interface, a multiple -pathway architecture that maintains 359 

performance regardless of whether experimental structural data is available, and the generation of 360 

drug -like candidates with QED scores substantially exceeding t hose of reference ligands. Future 361 

development will focus on systematic benchmarking against experimental binding data, 362 

incorporation of more rigorous binding affinity estimation methods, expansion to additional 363 

therapeutic modalities, automated candidate docking for additional binder screening, and broader 364 

validation across diverse target classes. 365 

F.A.D.E. is openly available on GitHub (https://github.com/Naveen -R-M/F.A.D.E), and we invite 366 

the community to contribute to its continued development. 367

of interest. In Step 1, a hierarchical database search determines the appropriate pathway based on data availability: (i) if a

ligand -bound structure with known binders is retriev ed from the RCSB PDB, the pipeline proceeds directly to candidate

predicted using Boltz -2. In Step 2, the identified or predicted binding site is used as input for de novo molecule generati on 

via DiffSBDD, an equivariant diffusion model conditioned on pocket geom etry. In Step 3, generated candidates are ranked 

by QSAR properties, Quantitative Estimate of Drug -likeness (QED), synthetic accessibility (SA) score, and predicted 

binding affinity estimated by Boltz -2, yielding a prioritised set of hit compounds.
