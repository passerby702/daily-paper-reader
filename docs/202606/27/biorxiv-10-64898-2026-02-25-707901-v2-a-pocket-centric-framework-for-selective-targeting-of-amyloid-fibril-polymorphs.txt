> Abstract 1
> The rapid expansion of high-resolution cryo-EM structures of amyloid fibrils has transformed our 2
> understanding of fibril polymorphism, yet it has not been matched by comparable progress in the rational 3
> development of protein-selective or polymorph-specific amyloid ligands. One possible explanation is that 4
> ligand selectivity is governed not only by global fibril folds, but also by local surface pockets accessible to 5
> small molecules. Here, we present a systematic analysis of 400 cryo-EM structures of amyloid-β, tau, and 6
> α-synuclein fibrils. Using a unified pocket similarity index and minimum spanning tree representations, we 7
> construct global and protein-specific graph representations of the amyloid binding pocket space, and 8
> examine how surface cavities are distributed across proteins, polymorphs, and structural contexts.  We 9
> find that many detectable pockets are shared across multiple fibrillar folds and, in several cases, across 10
> distinct amyloid-forming proteins, suggesting that pocket-level convergence may contribute to the limited 11
> selectivity of amyloid-directed ligands. Conversely, only a restricted subset of pockets occupies isolated 12
> regions  of  pocket  similarity  space,  defining  rare  structural  opportunities  for  protein-selective  or 13
> polymorph-restricted targeting. Analysis of structures of extracted fibrils further shows that disease-14
> derived fibril pockets do not form a completely isolated pocketome subset, but can resemble pockets 15
> observed in selected  in vitro  polymorphs. Together, these results reframe amyloid ligand development as 16
> a problem of pocket-level discriminability within a constrained fibril landscape, and provide a structural 17
> framework for identifying promising binding sites while avoiding intrinsically non-discriminatory pockets. 18
> Significance Statement 19
> Despite major advances in cryo-EM structure determination of amyloid fibrils, the development of 20
> selective ligands for amyloid assemblies remains challenging. By systematically comparing surface binding 21
> pockets across 400 amyloid-β, tau, and α-synuclein fibrillar structures, we show that many ligand-22
> accessible cavities exhibit similar geometric and physicochemical properties across fibrillar polymorphs 23
> made of distinct proteins. This pocket-level convergence provides a structural basis for understanding 24
> why many amyloid ligands exhibit broad binding profiles, while also identifying rare pockets that are 25
> sufficiently isolated to support more selective targeting strategies. Our work establishes a pocket-centric 26
> framework for interpreting amyloid ligand selectivity and for prioritizing fibril binding sites in imaging and 27
> therapeutic ligand development. 28

> Introduction 32
> 33
> Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s diseases are the most prevalent neurodegenerative disorders and represent a 34
> growing global health burden. As of 2023, more than 40 million individuals were diagnosed with 35
> Alzheimer’s disease (AD), the vast majority of whom were over 60 years of age (1). Clinically, AD is 36
> characterized by progressive cognitive decline, while Parkinson’s disease (PD), the most common 37
> neurodegenerative movement disorder, presents with motor symptoms such as rigidity, tremor, and 38
> bradykinesia, alongside a wide spectrum of non-motor manifestations (2). 39
> 40
> Although genetic risk factors have been identified for both disorders (3–5), approximately 90–95% of 41
> cases are sporadic, possibly involving environmental stressors (6). At the neuropathological level, AD and 42
> PD share key features, including neuroinflammation and the accumulation of proteinaceous aggregates. 43
> AD is characterized by extracellular amyloid plaques composed of amyloid-β peptides and intracellular 44
> neurofibrillary tangles enriched in tau protein (7). This places AD within the broader class of tauopathies, 45
> which also includes chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), frontotemporal dementia (FTD), corticobasal 46
> degeneration  (CBD),  and  progressive  supranuclear  palsy  (PSP)  (8).  PD  belongs  to  the  group  of 47
> synucleinopathies, which includes disorders such as dementia with Lewy bodies (DLB), multiple system 48

> atrophy (MSA), and juvenile-onset synucleinopathy (JOS), all linked to the aggregation of α-synuclein into 49
> amyloid fibrils that form together with membranous components Lewy bodies and neurites (9, 10). 50
> 51
> Importantly, the accumulation of amyloid fibrils in the brain precedes the onset of clinical symptoms by 52
> many years, and in some cases by decades, making these aggregates prime targets for early diagnosis and 53
> disease monitoring (11, 12). The development of cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) has enabled the 54
> determination of amyloid fibril structures at near-atomic resolution, revealing an unexpected degree of 55
> structural polymorphism. Both  in vitro  assembled recombinant proteins and fibrils extracted from patient 56
> brain tissue have shown that a given protein can adopt distinct fibrillar folds depending on environmental 57
> conditions or disease context (13–52). Such different polymorphs were also shown to trigger distinct 58
> diseases when delivered to animal models (53–57). In particular, fibrils extracted from patients affected 59
> by different tauopathies or synucleinopathies exhibit disease-specific folds, suggesting a structure– 60
> pathology relationship (58–63). 61
> 62
> Despite this wealth of structural information, the rational development of ligands capable of selectively 63
> targeting specific amyloid proteins, or specific fibrillar polymorphs of the same protein, remains highly 64
> challenging. Positron emission tomography (PET) has enabled the  in vivo  detection of amyloid deposits, 65
> and several tracers have been approved or advanced in clinical development for amyloid-β and tau 66
> imaging (64–66). More recently, α-synuclein tracers such as [¹⁸F]ACI-12589 have shown promise for 67
> imaging multiple system atrophy (67). Nevertheless, many amyloid-directed ligands display broad binding 68
> profiles across fibril types, limited discrimination between polymorphs, or off-target interactions that 69
> restrict their diagnostic and therapeutic utility (68, 69). 70
> 71
> This difficulty should not be interpreted as a simple consequence of insufficient structural information. 72
> Amyloid ligand design faces several intrinsic challenges: (i) high-resolution fibril structures have only 73
> recently become available at scale; (ii) they often lack structural information about flexible regions 74
> spanning significant fractions of their constitutive proteins; (iii) the fraction of disease-associated fibrils 75
> extracted from patients’ brains whose structures have been solved are often difficult to generate  de nov o76
> (28, 44); and (iv) the quantitative binding assays for filamentous assemblies remain more complex than 77
> assays on soluble globular proteins. Moreover, the few cryo-EM structures of amyloid fibrils in complex 78
> with small molecules suggest that ligand recognition can differ substantially from classical binding to a 79
> pre-formed protein cavity. In several cases, ligands bind through repeated or stacked arrangements along 80
> the fibril axis, combining fibril–ligand and ligand–ligand interactions (33, 48, 49, 70). 81
> 82
> Within  this  broader  context,  we  focus  here  on  one  structural  determinant  that  has  remained 83
> comparatively  underexplored:  the  local  surface  pockets  exposed  by  amyloid  fibrils.  While  fibril 84
> polymorphs are usually classified according to their global fold, small-molecule recognition is governed by 85
> the geometry and physicochemical properties of local binding environments. Thus, structurally distinct 86
> fibril folds may expose similar ligand-accessible pockets, whereas related folds may present different local 87
> binding sites. This raises a central question: does amyloid fibril polymorphism generate sufficiently 88
> distinct pocket landscapes to support protein-selective or polymorph-specific ligand design? 89
> 90
> To address this question, we adopt a pocket-centric perspective and perform a systematic pocketome 91
> analysis of amyloid fibrils. Pocketomes were originally developed to catalogue and compare ligand-92
> binding cavities in proteins and protein–protein interaction interfaces (71–74), providing a quantitative 93
> framework to compare binding environments independently of global fold similarity. Here, we extend this 94
> concept to amyloid assemblies by analyzing surface pockets across all cryo-EM structures of amyloid-β, 95
> tau, and α-synuclein fibrils available in the Protein Data Bank as of February 2026. Importantly, our aim is 96
> not to assign equal biological relevance to all  in vitro , seeded, mutant, extracted and ligand-bound 97
> structures, but to map the structural envelope of pocket diversity across experimentally observed fibrillar 98

> By detecting surface-accessible pockets, comparing them through a unified pocket similarity index, and 102
> visualizing their relationships using minimum spanning tree representations, we construct global and 103
> protein-specific amyloid pocketomes. This framework enables us to identify broadly recurrent pockets 104
> that are likely to support non-discriminatory binding, as well as rare isolated pockets that may offer more 105
> realistic opportunities for selective targeting. In doing so, we reframe amyloid ligand development not as 106
> a direct consequence of fibril fold classification, but as a problem of pocket-level discriminability within a 107
> constrained landscape of amyloid surface cavities. 108

> Results 110
> 111
> Detection of surface-accessible pockets across amyloid fibril polymorphs 112
> To  systematically  compare  ligand-relevant  binding  environments  across  amyloid  fibrils,  we  first 113
> established a unified workflow for structure selection, pocket detection and pocket filtering. We retrieved 114
> 400 cryo-EM structures of α-synuclein, tau, and amyloid-β fibrils available in the Protein Data Bank as of 115
> February 2026, including  in vitro  assembled fibrils, filaments purified from patients’ brain homogenates, 116
> and seed amplification assay products (Fig. 2, Fig. S1-S3 and Supplementary Files 1-3). 117
> 118
> Because many deposited fibril structures correspond to closely related or identical folds, analyzing each 119
> entry independently would introduce substantial redundancy into the pocketome. We therefore classified 120
> structures within each amyloid protein family using the Calypso workflow developed by  Connor et al. ,121
> based on fold-level RMSD relationships (52, 75). Distance thresholds were selected to define a tractable 122
> set of representative polymorph groups for each protein, yielding approximately 15 groups per amyloid 123
> family  (Fig.  3;  Fig.  S4–S5).  These  groupings  were  broadly  consistent  with  previously  reported 124
> classifications of amyloid fibril polymorphs (52, 76–78). For each group, we selected the best-resolved 125
> representative structure for downstream pocket analysis (Fig. S6–S8). Two α-synuclein entries, 7YK8 and 126
> 9JI8, were excluded because their short, flat, and partially unresolved peptide segments did not provide 127
> suitable fibrillar models for pocket detection. For Pick’s disease tau filaments, 6GX5 was retained as the 128
> representative structure because the better-resolved entry 8P34 contains only a single chain in the 129
> deposited PDB model, precluding reliable pocket detection on a fibrillar assembly. 130
> 131
> Surface-accessible cavities were then detected on each representative fibril using VolSite (79) (Fig. 4A–C). 132
> Because amyloid fibrils contain grooves, inter-protofilament interfaces, buried channels, and fibril-end 133
> cavities that are not all equally relevant to small-molecule recognition, we applied stringent filtering 134
> criteria to retain only pockets that are exposed at the fibril surface and repeated along the fibril axis. 135
> Cavities confined to fibril tips, buried within the fibril core, located at protofilament interfaces, too small, 136
> or geometrically constrained were excluded (Fig. 4C; Fig. S9). This procedure was designed to focus the 137
> analysis on recurring surface environments that could plausibly contribute to ligand binding, rather than 138
> on inaccessible or artefactual cavities. 139
> 140
> As an internal validation of this detection strategy, we also applied the same VolSite procedure to 141
> available cryo-EM structures of ligand-bound amyloid fibrils. The detected cavities overlapped well with 142
> the experimentally reported ligand-binding regions described in the corresponding structural studies (Fig. 143
> S10), supporting the relevance of the filtered pockets as ligand-accessible surface environments. 144
> 145
> The resulting set of pockets constitutes the fundamental unit of the amyloid pocketome. Each retained 146
> pocket was encoded using a common descriptor space capturing geometric and physicochemical 147
> properties, enabling quantitative comparison of binding environments across proteins, polymorphs, and 148
> structural contexts (Fig. 4D). 149

> The global amyloid pocketome reveals extensive cross-protein pocket similarity 151
> We next asked whether amyloid fibrils formed by different proteins expose distinct or overlapping ligand-152
> accessible pocket landscapes. To address this question, all retained surface pockets from amyloid-β, tau, 153
> and α-synuclein fibrils were compared within a unified descriptor space. Pairwise pocket similarity was 154
> quantified using the Pocket Similarity Index (PSI) (Equation 1), and the resulting relationships were 155
> visualized as a minimum spanning tree in which each node represents a pocket, and neighboring nodes 156
> correspond to locally similar binding environments (Fig. 5). 157
> 158
> If protein identity were the dominant determinant of pocket architecture, pockets from amyloid-β, tau, 159
> and α-synuclein would be expected to occupy distinct regions of the tree. Instead, the global amyloid 160
> pocketome forms a largely continuous landscape in which pockets from the three amyloid-forming 161
> proteins are extensively intermingled. Although local enrichments of protein-associated pockets can be 162
> observed, these regions do not form globally isolated branches. Rather, they remain embedded within a 163
> shared pocket similarity space populated by pockets from multiple amyloid proteins. 164
> 165
> This organization indicates that amyloid fibrils with distinct sequences and global folds can nevertheless 166
> expose locally similar surface cavities. Thus, protein identity and fibril fold are not sufficient to define 167
> ligand-relevant binding environments. At the pocket level, many amyloid fibrils converge toward similar 168
> combinations of size, shape, accessibility, and physicochemical properties. This convergence is particularly 169
> important because small-molecule recognition is governed by the local environment encountered by the 170
> ligand, rather than by the overall fibril architecture. 171
> 172
> The extensive intermixing observed in the global pocketome provides a structural rationale for the broad 173
> binding  profiles  frequently  reported  for amyloid-directed  ligands.  Pockets  located  within  densely 174
> populated regions of the tree are not unique to a single amyloid protein, and ligands targeting such sites 175
> would be expected to display limited protein selectivity. Conversely, only pockets occupying more isolated 176
> regions of the pocketome would be expected to offer realistic opportunities for selective ligand 177
> recognition. These results therefore establish pocket-level convergence as a major structural constraint 178
> on amyloid ligand selectivity. 179
> 180
> Pocket similarity is decoupled from fibril fold and polymorph classification 181
> We next examined whether pocket similarity follows the polymorph classification derived from fibril fold 182
> comparisons. When the global amyloid pocketome was colored by polymorph group identity, pockets 183
> from the same fold-defined group did not systematically co-localize within the tree (Fig. S11). Instead, 184
> pockets from distinct polymorphs frequently occupied neighboring positions, whereas different pockets 185
> from the same polymorph were often distributed across distant branches. Thus, a fibril polymorph does 186
> not correspond to a single, homogeneous pocket environment, but rather exposes multiple surface 187
> cavities with distinct similarity relationships. 188
> 189
> To determine whether this apparent decoupling persists within individual amyloid protein families, we 190
> generated protein-specific pocketomes for α-synuclein, tau, and amyloid-β (Fig. 6; Fig. S12–S13). This 191
> analysis directly addresses whether polymorph-restricted pocket environments emerge once cross-192
> protein diversity is removed. Across all three proteins, the pocketomes remained heterogeneous and only 193
> weakly structured by fold-defined polymorph identity. In the α-synuclein pocketome, pockets from the 194
> different polymorph groups were broadly distributed throughout the tree, with no dominant branches 195
> corresponding to individual polymorphs (Fig. 6). Similar patterns were observed for tau and amyloid-β, 196
> including disease-associated folds, whose pockets remained interspersed with pockets from other 197
> structural contexts rather than forming isolated regions (Fig. S12–S13). 198

> These observations demonstrate that pocket similarity is not a direct consequence of fibril fold similarity. 200
> Structurally distinct polymorphs can expose locally similar surface pockets, while pockets from the same 201
> polymorph can differ substantially from one another. This decoupling has important implications for 202
> amyloid ligand design. A ligand selected against one pocket of a given fibril polymorph may recognize 203
> similar pockets present in other polymorphs, whereas other pockets on the same fibril may not be 204
> recognized at all. As a result, polymorph-specific targeting cannot be inferred from fold classification 205
> alone. 206
> 207
> Recurrent sequence motifs underlie non-discriminatory pocket classes 208
> To better understand why many pockets occupy shared regions of pocketome space, we annotated each 209
> pocket according to the amino-acid residues contributing to its boundaries. This residue-level analysis 210
> showed that recurrent pocket classes are often defined by short local motifs rather than by the global 211
> architecture of the fibril. In particular, highly connected regions of the pocketome frequently correspond 212
> to shallow cavities formed by neighboring charged, polar, or aromatic residues, including lysines, 213
> arginines, glutamines, and tyrosines. 214
> 215
> In α-synuclein, several recurrent pocket classes are formed between closely spaced basic residues, 216
> including K21–K23, K43–K45, and K58–K60. These sites define small and elongated surface cavities, 217
> typically with volumes in the range of 100–150 Å³. Their chemical composition may provide favorable 218
> electrostatic or hydrogen-bonding opportunities, but their limited size and shallow geometry impose 219
> strong constraints on ligand accommodation. Such pockets are therefore unlikely to support highly 220
> selective recognition by a single small molecule unless additional interactions, such as repeated binding 221
> along the fibril axis or ligand–ligand stacking, contribute to binding. 222
> 223
> This residue-level view explains why pocket similarity can be partly decoupled from fibril fold similarity. 224
> Distinct fibril architectures can repeatedly expose comparable local arrangements of side chains, 225
> generating pockets that appear similar to a ligand even when the underlying backbone folds differ. 226
> Conversely, different surface regions of the same fibril can generate chemically and geometrically distinct 227
> pockets. Thus, pocket recurrence arises from local residue environments rather than from polymorph 228
> identity alone. 229
> 230
> These  recurrent  local  motifs  represent  structurally  plausible  but  poorly  discriminating  binding 231
> environments. Their abundance across the pocketome suggests that many amyloid-directed ligands may 232
> bind broadly, not because they recognize a conserved global fibril fold, but because they engage common 233
> local surface motifs exposed by multiple amyloid assemblies. Identifying such motifs is therefore useful 234
> not only for explaining off-target binding, but also for deprioritizing pockets that are intrinsically unlikely 235
> to support protein-selective or polymorph-specific ligand design. 236
> 237
> Pocketome clustering identifies rare isolated pockets compatible with selective targeting 238
> Having established that most amyloid pockets occupy a continuous and partially overlapping similarity 239
> landscape, we next sought to identify the unique pockets that might support selective recognition. We 240
> reasoned that a pocket compatible with protein-selective or polymorph-restricted ligand design should 241
> satisfy two conditions: it should be defined by a unique binding environment, and it should be globally 242
> isolated from unrelated pockets, so that ligands targeting it are less likely to cross-react with other 243
> amyloid assemblies/polymorphs. 244
> 245
> To identify such environments, we applied density-based clustering to the PSI-derived distance matrices 246
> obtained from the global and protein-specific pocketomes. This analysis was not intended to assign 247
> biological function to individual pockets, but to distinguish compact and structurally discriminable pocket 248
> classes from the continuous background of weakly separated surface cavities (Fig. S14). Across the 249
> datasets, most pockets were not assigned to any cluster, consistent with the largely continuous 250

> detected, corresponding to recurrent pocket environments that remain relatively isolated from the rest of 252
> the pocketome. 253
> 254
> These isolated clusters fall into two main categories. Within the first, protein-conserved pockets, largely 255
> restricted to the amyloid-forming protein, are shared by several polymorphs of the considered protein. A 256
> representative example is amyloid cluster 6, composed exclusively of tau pockets (Fig. S15A). This cluster 257
> includes pockets from tau filaments extracted from Pick’s disease (6GX5), tau P301L and P301T mutant 258
> fibrils (9GG0 and 9GG1), CBD type II tau filaments (6TJX), CBD-seeded fibrils (8ORG), and  in vitro  fibrils 259
> formed from a partial tau construct in the presence of KCl (7R5H). Despite differences in global fibril fold, 260
> disease context, and assembly conditions, these pockets retain a similar local geometry, reflecting 261
> conserved side-chain arrangements. Such pockets may represent plausible targets for protein-selective 262
> but polymorph-agnostic ligands. 263
> 264
> The second category, termed “cross-amyloid pockets”, corresponds to structurally similar cavities found in 265
> different amyloid-forming proteins (Fig. S15B). Amyloid cluster 4, which includes small pockets of 266
> approximately 150 Å³ or less from extracted α-synuclein JOS fibrils (8BQV), limbic-predominant neuronal 267
> inclusion body 4R tauopathy type 1a tau filaments (7P6A), and AD-PHF-like  in vitro  tau fibrils (8Q8R) 268
> perfectly represents this category of pockets. These pockets are shallow, geometrically constrained, and 269
> chemically simple, often formed around adjacent lysine, arginine, glutamine, or glycine residues. Their 270
> recurrence  across  unrelated  amyloid  proteins  identifies  them  as  intrinsically  non-discriminatory 271
> environments. Although such pockets may support ligand binding, they are poor candidates for selective 272
> ligand design because structurally similar binding sites exist elsewhere in the amyloid pocketome. 273
> 274
> Notably, we did not identify compact clusters composed exclusively of multiple pockets from a given 275
> polymorph. This suggests that polymorph-specific binding environments are rare in the current structural 276
> dataset. However, some individual pockets occupy terminal positions in the pocketome tree and display 277
> very low similarity to the rest of the dataset, with PSI values below 0.005. These pockets represent 278
> structural outliers rather than recurrent clusters. They are often large or more complex cavities, defined 279
> by four or more contributing residues, and may therefore provide the most plausible starting points for 280
> polymorph-restricted ligand design (Fig. S15C). At the same time, their uniqueness also implies a more 281
> challenging ligand-design problem, because such pockets may be less repetitive, less shallow, and less 282
> chemically simple than the recurrent non-discriminatory pockets. 283
> 284
> Together, these analyses show that selective amyloid targeting is governed not by the mere presence of 285
> detectable pockets, but by their position within the global pocketome. Pockets embedded in densely 286
> populated regions are intrinsically prone to cross-reactivity, whereas isolated clusters or extreme outliers 287
> define the limited subset of binding environments for which selective recognition may be structurally 288
> plausible. 289

> Overlap between pockets within α-synuclein fibrils extracted from patients’ brains and selected in vitro 291
> assembled fibrils 292
> We next focused on α-synuclein fibrils, for which the relationship between  in vitro  assembly models and 293
> disease-derived structures remains a central issue for ligand discovery. Unlike several tau fibril folds, 294
> which have been reproduced  in vitro  (28) or by seeding from disease-derived material, α-synuclein fibrils 295
> extracted from the brain of patients developing distinct synucleinopathies remain difficult to recapitulate 296
> experimentally (44). Numerous  in vitro  assembly conditions generate distinct α-synuclein polymorphs, 297
> some of which induce very efficiently pathological phenotypes in cellular or animal models (53, 54), but 298
> their folds differ from those reported for fibrils extracted from the brain of a very limited number of MSA, 299
> PD, PDD patients. We therefore asked whether the latter fibrils expose pockets that are structurally 300

> The α-synuclein pocketome showed that disease-derived pockets do not form a completely isolated 304

> protofilament that is more distinct from the rest of the dataset (Fig. 3). At the pocket level, however, 307

> 309
> Two pockets from MSA type I protofilament IA illustrate this relationship (Fig. 7A). The first is a small N-310
> terminal pocket enriched in lysine residues, corresponding to pocket 15, which belongs to α-synuclein 311
> cluster 1 together with pockets from  in vitro  fibrils formed in the presence of ATP (9QYL) or induced by 312

> 319
> The situation is more nuanced for fibrils from PD/PDD/DLB patients (Fig. 7B). The two largest cavities 320
> detected in the PDD structure displayed highly distinctive geometries, with PSI values of 0.005 or lower 321
> relative to the rest of the pocketome. These pockets represent potential structural outliers, but should be 322
> interpreted cautiously as ligand-design opportunities. The largest cavity, located between K32 and K45, 323
> coincides with an unidentified density reported in the original cryo-EM map, while the second largest 324
> pocket is comparatively buried and poorly accessible. In contrast, the remaining smaller pockets from 325
> structures from PD/PDD/DLB patients were closer to pockets observed in other α-synuclein polymorphs. 326
> 327
> Together,  these  results  show  that  pockets  within  patient-derived  α-synuclein  fibrils  are  neither 328
> completely distinct from, nor broadly shared with,  in vitro  fibrillar polymorphs.. Some disease-derived 329
> pockets overlap with binding environments found in selected  in vitro  fibrils, supporting the use of 330
> carefully chosen  in vitro  polymorphs as structural models for ligand discovery. At the same time, isolated 331
> pockets within fibrils extracted from patients may provide opportunities for more selective targeting, but 332
> only if their accessibility, repeatability along the fibril axis, and compatibility with ligand binding can be 333
> established experimentally. This pocket-centric view therefore provides a more nuanced framework than 334
> global fold comparison alone for selecting α-synuclein fibril models in ligand-design studies. 335
> 336
> Discussion 337
> 338
> Amyloid ligand selectivity is constrained by pocket-level convergence 339
> The  rapid  expansion  of  high-resolution  amyloid  fibril  structures  has  profoundly  changed  our 340
> understanding  of  fibril  polymorphism  and  disease-associated  amyloid  strains  (62).  However,  this 341
> structural progress has not yet translated into a comparable ability to design ligands that selectively 342
> recognize a given amyloid protein, disease-associated fold, or fibril polymorph (69). The present study 343
> provides a structural explanation for this disconnect. By comparing ligand-accessible surface pockets 344
> across amyloid-β, tau, and α-synuclein fibrils, we show that local binding environments are far less diverse 345
> than the fibril folds that display them. 346
> 347
> The main conclusion of this work is that amyloid polymorphism does not automatically generate pocket-348
> level discriminability. Fibrils that differ in sequence, global architecture, protofilament arrangement, or 349
> disease context can nevertheless expose surface cavities with similar size, shape, accessibility, and 350
> physicochemical properties. Conversely, different pockets on the same fibril can occupy distant regions of 351
> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 26, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.02.25.707901 doi: bioRxiv preprint 9
> pocket similarity space. Thus, the structural unit that matters for ligand recognition is not the fibril fold as 352
> a whole, but the local pocket accessible to the ligand. 353
> 354
> This observation reframes a long-standing problem in amyloid ligand development. Broad binding profiles 355
> and off-target recognition are often interpreted primarily as limitations of ligand chemistry. Our results 356
> suggest that they can also arise from the pocket landscape itself. If a ligand targets a cavity embedded in a 357
> densely populated region of amyloid pocketome space, structurally similar binding environments are 358
> likely to exist on other proteins or polymorphs. In such cases, improving ligand affinity may not be 359
> sufficient to achieve selectivity, because the underlying pocket is intrinsically non-discriminatory. 360
> 361
> Recurrent local environments explain why specificity is rare 362
> The residue-level analysis helps explain why pocket-level convergence is so widespread. Many recurrent 363
> pockets are built from short local arrangements of charged, polar, or aromatic residues that are 364
> repeatedly  exposed  on  fibril  surfaces.  These  motifs  can  generate  chemically  attractive  binding 365
> environments, but they are often shallow, small, and geometrically constrained. Such pockets may 366
> support amyloid ligand binding, especially when binding is reinforced by repetition along the fibril axis or 367
> by ligand–ligand stacking, but they provide limited structural information for discriminating between 368
> related amyloid assemblies. 369
> 370
> This distinction is important because a pocket can be both suited for binding and poorly selective. In 371
> classical structure-based ligand design, the presence of a well-defined cavity is often taken as a favorable 372
> starting point. In amyloid fibrils however, the relevant question is not only whether a pocket can 373
> accommodate a ligand, but whether that pocket is sufficiently distinct from the rest of the amyloid 374
> pocketome. A shallow lysine- or glutamine-rich groove may be chemically accessible, yet if comparable 375
> grooves occur repeatedly across unrelated fibrils, ligands directed toward such sites are expected to 376
> display broad recognition profiles. 377
> 378
> The rare pockets that appear most compatible with selective targeting are therefore not necessarily the 379
> most obvious or abundant cavities. Rather, they are pockets that are isolated in pocket similarity space, 380
> either as compact protein-restricted clusters or as individual structural outliers. Protein-conserved 381
> pockets may be useful for designing ligands that recognize a class of fibrils, such as tau or α-synuclein 382
> assemblies, without distinguishing every polymorph. In contrast, polymorph-restricted targeting would 383
> require pockets that are not only accessible and repeated along the fibril axis but also sufficiently distinct 384
> from all other amyloid pockets. Our analysis suggests that such pockets are uncommon in the current 385
> structural dataset. The reason behind this finding is unclear and may be related to fibrils’ helicity that 386
> constrains key residues' folding events. Fibrillar polymorphs lacking helical symmetry, such as ribbons 387
> (13), are not represented within the PDB and could well help to identify unique binding opportunities. 388
> 389
> Implications for in vitro models, extracted fibrils, and ligand discovery 390
> The pocketome framework also provides a practical way to reassess the value of  in vitro  fibril models for 391
> amyloid ligand discovery.  In vitro  assemblies are often considered imperfect models of disease because 392
> their global folds can differ from those extracted from patients. This concern is particularly acute for α-393
> synuclein, where disease-derived fibrils remain difficult to reproduce experimentally. Our results suggest 394
> that the relevance of an  in vitro  model should not be judged only by global fold similarity, but also by 395
> whether it reproduces the local pocket environment that a ligand is intended to target. 396
> 397
> This distinction is important for structure-guided ligand design. An  in vitro  fibril that does not match an 398
> extracted fold globally may still expose a pocket comparable to a disease-derived binding site and may 399
> therefore serve as a useful model for studying ligand recognition. Conversely, an  in vitro  fibril that appears 400
> globally related to a disease-associated structure may expose a different set of surface cavities and may 401
> be less informative for ligand optimization. A pocket-centric comparison can therefore guide the selection 402
> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 26, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.02.25.707901 doi: bioRxiv preprint 10
> of experimental fibril models by matching the local binding environment rather than relying exclusively on 403
> fold classification. 404
> 405
> Analysis of fibrillar α-synuclein polymorphs illustrates this point. Some pockets detected in fibrils 406
> extracted from MSA patients overlap with pockets found in selected  in vitro  polymorphs, including ATP- or 407
> heparin-associated fibrils and ligand-bound WT-polymorph 5A fibrils. These relationships do not imply 408
> that the  in vitro  structures reproduce the full disease-associated fibril fold. Rather, they indicate that 409
> specific local binding environments can be shared between  in vitro  and extracted polymorphs. Such 410
> overlap may justify the use of selected  in vitro  fibrils as tractable structural models for ligand discovery, 411
> provided that the targeted pocket is the relevant shared feature. 412
> 413
> At the same time, the pocketome highlights pockets that should be deprioritized. Cross-amyloid pockets, 414
> especially small and shallow cavities formed by recurrent local motifs, may be attractive from a purely 415
> chemical perspective but are intrinsically prone to off-target binding. For ligand development, excluding 416
> such non-discriminatory pockets may be as important as identifying promising ones. In this sense, the 417
> amyloid pocketome can function as a negative-design tool: it helps identify binding environments where 418
> selectivity is unlikely to emerge, even after extensive chemical optimization. 419
> 420
> Such observations partially match the conclusions of  Sadek et al.  (preprint, 80) , who developed a 421
> computational pipeline entitled FibrilSite to identify interaction sites at the surface of amyloid fibrils, 422
> compare their properties and determine the most druggable sites for ligand development prioritization. 423
> The method we present here and that by  Sadek et al. , shows overlap, in particular for fibrillar α-synuclein 424
> polymorphs made  in vitro  or extracted from MSA patients. However, fewer but larger sites were identified 425
> by the latter method, leading to the conclusion that no similar sites exist between amyloid-forming 426
> proteins, in contradiction with the observed lack of selectivity of available and developed ligands. 427
> 428
> For diagnostic imaging, the desired selectivity profile will depend on the intended application. Despite 429
> limiting the design of ligands that discriminate between closely related pathological strains, the presence 430
> of pockets shared across multiple polymorphs is of interest. Such pockets allow the binding of ligands 431
> targeting a broader class of assemblies, such as pan-synucleinopathy or pan-tauopathy tracers and/or co-432
> pathologies such as those observed in DLB or PSP and brain region-based disease diagnosis and follow-up. 433
> In contrast, tracers intended to distinguish disease subtypes or fibril strains would require pockets that 434
> are both accessible and isolated from the broader pocketome. The present analysis suggests that the 435
> latter case is structurally much more constrained and will require careful prioritization of rare pocket 436
> outliers rather than broadly recurrent cavities. These considerations therefore reinforce the need to 437
> evaluate amyloid targets at the level of pocket discriminability rather than at the level of fibril fold identity 438
> alone. 439
> 440
> Limitations and outlook 441
> 442
> Several limitations should be considered when interpreting this pocketome analysis. First, the detection of 443
> a surface pocket does not demonstrate ligand binding, high affinity, or selectivity. The pockets identified 444
> here are structural environments that are accessible and repeated along fibril surfaces, but their 445
> functional relevance must be established experimentally. Pocket isolation in similarity space should be 446
> viewed as a prioritization criterion rather than as a direct prediction of ligandability. 447
> 448
> Second, amyloid ligand binding can differ substantially from classical small-molecule recognition by 449
> globular proteins. In several cryo-EM structures of ligand-bound amyloid fibrils, ligands bind in repeated 450
> arrays along the fibril axis and may be stabilized by both fibril–ligand and ligand–ligand interactions. Such 451
> cooperative or stacking effects are not fully captured by pocket descriptors based on local cavity 452
> .CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 26, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.02.25.707901 doi: bioRxiv preprint 11
> geometry and physicochemical properties. As a result, the pocketome provides information on the local 453
> binding environment, but not on the full thermodynamic or supramolecular binding mechanism. 454
> 455
> Third, amyloid fibril structures usually resolve only the ordered fibrillar core. Flexible N- or C-terminal 456
> regions, post-translational modifications, cofactors, lipids, and other cellular components may contribute 457
> to ligand recognition i n vivo  but are absent or incompletely represented in most structural models. This is 458
> particularly relevant for α-synuclein and tau, whose disordered regions may influence accessibility, 459
> surface chemistry, interactions with other molecules or even define additional unforeseen binding sites. 460
> Future analyses integrating cryo-EM density, molecular dynamics, and experimentally mapped ligand-461
> binding data will be needed to refine the relationship between detected pockets and true binding sites. 462
> 463
> Finally, the pocketome is limited by the structural diversity available in the Protein Data Bank at the time 464
> of analysis. As additional extracted polymorphs’ structures, in situ ligand-bound complexes, seeded fibrils, 465
> and disease-associated fibrillar polymorphs whose structures will be solved in situ, become available, the 466
> pocket similarity landscape will need to be updated. This is not a weakness of the framework, but one of 467
> its main advantages: newly solved structures can be directly placed within the existing pocketome to 468
> determine whether they introduce novel binding environments or recapitulate already populated regions 469
> of amyloid pocket space. 470
> 471
> Despite these limitations, the present study establishes a general framework for evaluating amyloid ligand 472
> targets in a comparative structural context. Rather than treating each fibril structure as an isolated object, 473
> the pocketome places every candidate binding site in relation to the full repertoire of known amyloid 474
> surface environments. This enables a more realistic assessment of which pockets are likely to support 475
> broad binding or genuine polymorph-restricted recognition. 476
> 477
> Concluding perspective 478
> 479
> Overall, this work argues for a shift from a fold-centric to pocket-centric paradigm in amyloid ligand 480
> discovery.  Fibril  polymorphism  remains  essential  for  understanding  amyloid  biology  and  disease 481
> heterogeneity, but ligand selectivity is ultimately governed by the local binding environments exposed on 482
> fibril surfaces. By systematically mapping these environments across amyloid-β, tau, and α-synuclein 483
> fibrils, we show that many pockets are shared across proteins and polymorphs, whereas truly isolated 484
> pockets are rare. 485
> 486
> This constrained pocket landscape helps explain why selective amyloid ligands have been difficult to 487
> obtain despite the growing availability of high-resolution fibril structures. It also provides a practical 488
> framework for future design efforts: prioritize pockets that are isolated in pocketome space, use  in vitro 489
> fibrils when they reproduce the relevant local binding environment, and avoid recurrent cross-amyloid 490
> pockets that are structurally predisposed to off-target recognition. In this sense, the amyloid pocketome 491
> does not merely catalogue fibril cavities; it defines the structural limits and opportunities for selective 492
> amyloid targeting. 493

> Creation of the dataset 497
> Atomic structures of amyloid fibrils were retrieved from the Amyloid Atlas (81, 82) using Calypso scraping 498
> algorithm (52, 75). Only structures determined by cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) were considered. 499
> ssNMR assemblies, and monomeric and peptide structures were excluded. Both apo fibrils and fibrils 500
> resolved in complex with small molecules were retained. 501
> 502
> Structural classification of fibril polymorphs 503

> To classify fibril polymorphs independently of pocket analysis, pairwise structural comparisons were 504
> performed separately for α-synuclein, tau, and amyloid-β fibrils using the Calypso workflow from  Connor 505
> et al.  (52, 75). The resulting hierarchical classification was used to obtain polymorph groups by manually 506
> selecting a proper RMSD distance. The chosen distances were of 5.3, 4.8 and 3.3 Å for α-synuclein, tau 507
> and amyloid-β structures respectively. Polymorph group assignments were then used for comparative 508
> analysis with pocketome organization. 509
> 510
> Preparation of amyloid fibril structures 511
> Each retained structure (polymorph group representatives and ligand-fibril complexes) was prepared 512
> using the Molecular Operating Environment (MOE) software (Chemical Computing Group). Preparation 513
> was performed using the QuickPrep protocol with default parameters. To ensure uniformity across the 514
> dataset, a maximum of four monomer layers was retained for each fibril, and no energy minimization or 515
> ligand refinement was applied to ligand-bound structures. Prepared structures were exported in MOL2 516
> format for pocket detection. 517
> 518
> Detection and filtering of surface pockets 519
> Surface pockets were detected on each prepared fibril structure using the VolSite algorithm implemented 520
> in the IChem toolkit. Pocket detection parameters were set according to those previously validated for 521
> protein–protein interaction cavities. 522
> Detected pockets were filtered according to the following criteria: 523
> 1.  Pocket volume must exceed 80 Å³. 524
> 2.  Pockets located at the fibril tips (above the first or below the last retained monomer layer) were 525
> excluded. 526
> 3.  Pockets located at the interface between protofilaments were excluded, as these regions lack 527
> sufficient accessibility for ligand binding. 528
> 4.  Cavities buried within the fibril core or confined to narrow internal channels were excluded. 529
> 5.  Hollow regions lacking well-defined residue contributions, as inferred from electron density 530
> maps, were excluded. 531
> In fibrils composed of two identical protofilaments, symmetric copies of the same pocket were frequently 532
> detected. To avoid redundancy and prevent symmetry-driven bias in similarity calculations, only the larger 533
> of the two symmetric pockets was retained as a representative for subsequent analyses. 534
> 535
