Abstract 9

Cryogenic electron microscopy (cryo-EM) enables high-resolution structural determination of large macromolec-10 

ular complexes. However, the interpretability of cryo-EM maps is often hindered by substantial background noise 11 

and signal attenuation, which obscure structural details. Although existing post-processing methods can partially 12 

mitigate these artifacts, they typically su ff er from over-smoothing and lack reliable confidence estimation. Here, we 13 

present CryoDi ff , an uncertainty-aware di ff usion model for cryo-EM map enhancement. CryoDi ff employs a multi-14 

step di ff usion process to progressively denoise and restore high-resolution structural features. Importantly, CryoDi ff 15 

incorporates a voxel-wise confidence metric derived from Monte Carlo sampling. It unifies map enhancement and 16 

voxel-level uncertainty estimation within a di ff usion-based generative framework, representing the first approach to 17 

achieve such joint modeling for cryo-EM map enhancement. In comprehensive experiments, CryoDi ff markedly out-18 

performs existing methods in both map–model correlation and map interpretability, improving the average FS C 0.519 

metric by 0.356 Å over state-of-the-art approaches. When applied to de novo model building with ModelAngelo, 20 

CryoDi ff further increases model completeness by 5 .5%, exceeding the gains achieved by competing method. 21

Cryogenic electron microscopy (cryo-EM) has emerged as a powerful technique for determining the structures of 23 

macromolecular complexes in their native states. In the single-particle cryo-EM workflow, three-dimensional density 24 

maps are reconstructed from tens of thousands of particle images (Nogales and Scheres, 2015) to serve as a reference 25 

for atomic model building. However, raw experimental maps frequently su ff er from contrast loss and high-frequency 26 

signal attenuation (Rosenthal and Henderson, 2003) due to factors such as intrinsic structural flexibility, radiation dam-27 

age (Glaeser and Taylor, 1978), and cumulative errors during the 3D reconstruction process. Such signal degradation 28 

obscures critical structural features, introducing significant ambiguity into backbone tracing and side-chain assign-29 

ment. Consequently, various post-processing approaches have been developed to recover these missing details and 30 

maximize map interpretability. 31 

Traditional map sharpening methods aim to compensate for the contrast loss in cryo-EM maps by amplifying high-32 

frequency Fourier components, which is typically achieved either by applying a global B-factor (Kimanius et al. , 2016; 33 

Zivanov et al. , 2018; Terwilliger et al. , 2018) or employing local sharpening techniques that leverage prior informa-34 

tion (Ram´ ırez-Aportela et al. , 2020; Jakobi et al. , 2017). In addition, density modification methods improve cryo-EM 35 

maps by incorporating prior knowledge, such as solvent characteristics or local noise distributions (Terwilliger et al. ,36 

2020a,b). These approaches employ physically or statistically grounded algorithms to enhance cryo-EM maps, provid-37 

ing consistent and reliable processed densities. Consequently, these methods have been widely adopted by the cryo-38 

EM community as standard procedures in the post-processing of cryo-EM maps, serving as important steps for both 39 

map deposition and downstream structural analysis. Despite their widespread adoption, these conventional approaches 40 

su ff er from notable limitations. Global B-factor-based sharpening cannot account for spatially varying signal-to-noise 41 

ratios, often leading to over-sharpening in flexible regions and under-sharpening in stable cores. Meanwhile, the ef-42 

ficacy of many local sharpening methods is restricted by their reliance on additional inputs, such as segmentation 43 

masks or pre-existing atomic models, limiting their applicability to novel structures where such priors are unavailable 44 

or unreliable. 45 

Deep-learning-based methods have been proposed for automatic cryo-EM map enhancement. Early attempts like 46 

DeepEMhancer (Sanchez-Garcia et al. , 2021) employs 3D U-Nets to replicate enhanced map sharpened by traditional 47

et al. , 2023; Cao et al. , 2025; Zhang et al. , 2025; Selvaraj et al. , 2024) were trained on paired data consisting of 49

ing to improve Cryo-EM maps. However, EMReady and similar deterministic deep-learning-based approaches exhibit 52 

critical limitations. First, these deep-learning approaches operate as ”black boxes” that directly modify real-space den-53 

sities without providing explicit explanations or quantitative confidence measures for the enhanced features. Second, 54 

cryo-EM reconstructions inherently represent an ensemble of conformations due to molecular flexibility (Herzik et al. ,55 

2019). By relying on single-pass feedforward networks that regress to a single deterministic output, these methods 56 

tend to produce overly smoothed densities, often blurring high-frequency details. 57 

Here we propose CryoDi ff , an uncertainty-aware di ff usion model for cryo-EM map enhancement that employs 58 

a conditional denoising di ff usion probabilistic model (Ho et al. , 2020). Instead of regressing to a single solution, 59 

CryoDi ff learns the distribution of high-quality structures and iteratively refines details from random Gaussian noise. 60 

This gradual denoising framework provides two crucial advantages over deterministic methods. First, it naturally 61 

mitigates over-smoothing by avoiding direct regression to a single, averaged solution. Second, by modeling the full 62 

conditional distribution, CryoDi ff can generate a diverse ensemble of plausible reconstructions, thereby capturing 63 

the inherent uncertainty of the enhancement process. To quantify this uncertainty, CryoDi ff further introduces a 64 

novel voxel-wise reliability metric, which measures confidence in the recovered features by performing Monte Carlo 65 

sampling and evaluating density variations across the ensemble. 66 

To evaluate the performance of CryoDi ff , we conducted comprehensive experiments on a large-scale benchmark 67 

comprising 143 primary maps and 80 pairs of half maps with resolution ranging from 2 to 5 Å. The case results 68 

show that CryoDi ff produces maps with substantially enhanced interpretability, characterized by improved structural 69 

continuity and the recovery of intricate side-chain densities often obscured in experimental maps. We quantitatively 70 

assessed its performance using map-model correlation metrics, including map-model FS C 0.5, Q-score(P), Q-score(N), 71 

CC box , CC mask and CC peaks . Specifically, CryoDi ff boosts the average FS C 0.5 resolution to 3.045 Å (primary) and 72 

2.946 Å (half-maps), outperforming the state-of-the-art method by 0.356 Å and 0.337 Å, respectively. Furthermore, 73 

this superior map quality enables downstream tools, like phenix.map to model and ModelAngelo, to generate more 74

## 2 Results 78 

2.1 Overview of CryoDi ff 79 

The complete workflow of CryoDi ff , illustrated in Figure 1, consists of four main stages: data generation, model 80 

training, reverse inference, and uncertainty estimation. In the data generation stage, we first collected 980 experimen-81 

tal cryo-EM maps from the Electron Microscopy Data Bank (EMDB) and split them into training (737), validation 82 

(100), and test (143) sets. Subsequently, for the training set, simulated maps were generated from corresponding PDB 83 

(Berman et al. , 2000) models to serve as the ground-truth for model training. All maps were finally uniformly pro-84 

cessed by resampling to the voxel size of 1 Å, normalizing intensity values to the range [0, 1], and partitioning into 85 

boxes of size 48 3. In the model training stage, CryoDi ff , a di ff usion-based 3D U-Net (detailed in 3.3 and Supplemen-86 

tary Figure S4) (Rombach et al. , 2022a), is trained to predict the Gaussian noise added to simulated map boxes x0 at 87 

the random di ff usion timestep t, with the corresponding conditional experimental map box xc, as detailed in Section 88 

3.4. This model is optimized via a hybrid Smooth L1 loss and SSIM loss. In the reverse inference stage, the input 89 

map is first partitioned into overlapping boxes of size 48 3 with a stride of 16. The denoising di ff usion implicit model 90 

(DDIM) sampler then iteratively generates the enhanced boxes ˆ x0 over S steps through a reverse di ff usion process, 91 

which starts from random Gaussian noise and is conditioned on the corresponding input experimental boxes. 92 

These enhanced boxes are then assembled to form the complete, high-quality enhanced map. In the uncertainty 93 

estimation stage, CryoDi ff introduces a voxel-wise confidence metric, pMDD, to quantify the structural reliability of 94 

the enhanced map, as detailed in Section 3.6. 95 

2.2 Local enhanced confidence estimation 96 

Except for the enhancement of cryo-EM densities, the post-processing of cryo-EM reconstructions also involves the 97 

validation of the maps. Validation of cryo-EM maps aids the subsequent model-building process by providing quan-98 

titative metrics that indicate how likely a feature within the density map represents specific molecular components. 99

metric is directly attributed to CryoDi ff ’s paired training strategy, wherein high-quality map regions correlate with 102

The proposed pMDD is initially derived from voxel-wise mean absolute error (MAE) over multiple di ff usion sam-106 

ples, leveraging the intrinsic uncertainty of the di ff usion process. However, we find that the pMDD–resolvability 107 

relationship inferred from raw MAE is influenced by the global resolution of the map, limiting its interpretability 108 

across datasets. To address this issue, we introduce an uncertainty calibration strategy that aligns pMDD with estab-109 

lished measures of map resolvability. After calibration, pMDD provides a normalized and comparable metric across 110 

maps, while preserving the relative ordering of voxel-wise errors. As a result, the calibrated pMDD serves as a reliable 111 

proxy for local resolvability and further captures structural variability arising from flexibility, noise, and potential arti-112 

facts. Note that, after calibration, higher pMDD values correspond to better local resolvability and higher confidence, 113 

aligning its interpretation with Q-score. 114

romyces cerevisiae (EMD-31487, 3.81 Å) (Xu et al. , 2022) as a case study (Figure 2). Figure 2a demonstrated the 116 

deposited and CryoDi ff -processed map colored with pMDD values. In the deposited map, the A and N domains ex-117 

hibit poor resolution, attributed to their intrinsic flexibility and residual conformational heterogeneity. This underlying 118 

instability is also evidenced by their reduced local pMDD values, which are significantly lower than those of the stable 119 

transmembrane (TM) domain. Figure 2b illustrates two α-helical segments in the TM domain of Dnf1 at di ff erent 120 

contour level. Unlike EMReady2, which lacks a local quality metric, CryoDi ff provides a pMDD-colored confidence 121 

map that explicitly highlights regions of structural uncertainty. This crucial feature allows researchers to readily dis-122 

tinguish between well-resolved region and potential artifacts or ambiguous densities. A detailed comparison at the low 123 

contour level (black arrows) reveals three key advantages of CryoDi ff ’s pMDD metric. First, an ASN side chain ap-124 

pears erroneously extended in the EMReady2-processed map, representing a feature that can be easily misinterpreted. 125 

In contrast, CryoDi ff assigns moderate uncertainty to this region, suggesting that the feature may be misleading while 126 

interpretation. Second, the ARG side chain remains ambiguous in both maps, while CryoDi ff provides crucial local 127 

information by highlighting lower pMDD near the base of the side chain. Third, the pMDD metric e ff ectively identifies 128

3.3 Network architecture 299 

We employed a conditional di ff usion model (Rombach et al. , 2022b) to enhance cryo-EM density maps, leveraging its 300 

ability to overcome the blurring e ff ects of conventional denoising and recover fine structural details. Inspired by LDM 301 

(Rombach et al. , 2022a), we adopted its time-conditional 2D U-Net backbone and extended it into time- and map-302 

conditional 3D U-Net architecture to serve as our reverse denoising network. The overview of CryoDi ff is presented 303 

in Figure 1. This 3D U-Net network consists of a hierarchical architecture comprising four encoder stages, a central 304 

bottleneck, and four decoder stages, bridged by skip connections. To balance feature extraction capabilities, residual 305 

convolutional blocks are applied across all scales for local modeling, whereas global attention blocks are selectively 306 

integrated at deeper stages to capture long-range dependencies between the noisy input and the conditioning cryo-EM 307 

densities. The detail of the architecture is shown in Supplementary Figure S4. 308

3.8 Network implementation 409 

The proposed network was implemented in PyTorch 1.13.1 (CUDA 12.2) and trained on two NVIDIA A100 GPUs. 410 

To mitigate overfitting and enhance model generalization, data augmentation techniques, including random cropping 411 

and rotation, were applied during training. The network was optimized using the AdamW optimizer with a batch size 412 

of 32 and an initial learning rate of 10 −4. The learning rate was dynamically reduced by 10% upon a validation loss 413 

plateau of six epochs to ensure stable convergence. The model was totally trained for 300 epochs. 414

## 4 Discussion 422 

In this work, we proposed a di ff usion-based model named CryoDi ff for Cryo-EM map post-processing. The evalua-423 

tion results demonstrate that CryoDi ff consistently outperforms existing methods across multiple metrics. Specifically, 424 

maps processed by CryoDi ff exhibit superior backbone continuity and better-resolved side-chain density features, 425 

thereby directly facilitating more accurate downstream structural modeling. The observed improvements underscore 426 

the intrinsic advantages of di ff usion-based modeling. While conventional feed-forward methods often su ff er from 427 

over-smoothing and the generation of artifactual features, CryoDi ff overcomes these limitations by leveraging the it-428 

erative and probabilistic sampling inherent in di ff usion processes. This allows the model to progressively reconstruct 429 

plausible density distributions conditioned on the experimental data. Consequently, this generative mechanism ef-430 

fectively mitigates over-smoothing and reduces the risk of artifacts, providing a more faithful representation of the 431 

underlying biological structures. 432 

Furthermore, CryoDi ff provides a voxel-wise reliability metric, termed pMDD, which is derived from the mean ab-433 

solute error (MAE) across multiple di ff usion samples and subsequently calibrated to correct for resolution-dependent 434 

biases, enabling a more interpretable measure of local resolvability. While we have demonstrated its e ffi cacy in high-435 

lighting regions susceptible to misinterpretation and its correlation with local quality metrics such as Q-score, certain 436 

limitation should be considered. Namely, pMDD is influenced by multiple factors—including intrinsic structural flex-437 

ibility, local resolution, and training data representation—rather than a single source of uncertainty. Consequently, a 438 

high pMDD value indicates general uncertainty but does not determine its specific cause. 439

Despite its superior performance, CryoDi ff presents certain limitations. First, the model may exhibit limited e ffi -440 

cacy in resolving features underrepresented in the current training data, such as small ligands, glycosylation sites, and 441 

metal-coordination spheres (see Supplementary Figure S2a). Additionally, CryoDi ff may occasionally misidentify ex-442 

tremely weak density signals as noise, potentially leading to the loss of real structural information (see Supplementary 443 

Figure S2b). To mitigate this risk, manual inspection and cross-validation against the raw experimental map remain 444 

essential. Finally, the current implementation is computationally intensive (see Supplementary Figure S3). Thus, our 445 

future work will focus on optimizing inference strategies and exploring di ff usion distillation techniques to enhance 446 

computational e ffi ciency for large-scale applications. 447
