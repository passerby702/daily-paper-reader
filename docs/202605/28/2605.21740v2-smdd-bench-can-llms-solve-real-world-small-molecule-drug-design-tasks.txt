## Abstract 

LLM agents have incredible potential for scientific discovery applications. How-ever, the performance of LLM agents on real-world, small molecule drug design (SMDD) tasks across diverse chemistries and targets is unclear. Current eval-uation methods are either ad hoc, too simple for real-world discovery, limited in scale, or restricted to single-turn question answering. In effort to standardize the evaluation of LLM agents on small molecule design, we introduce SMDD-Bench, a challenging, multi-turn, long-horizon agentic benchmark consisting of 502 guaranteed-solvable task instances spanning 5 task types: 2D Pharmacophore Identification, Interaction Point Discovery, Scaffold Hopping, Lead Optimization, and Fragment Assembly. SMDD-Bench tasks span a wide region of chemical space and involve 102 unique protein targets. Completely solving the benchmark would require having strong chemical and biological reasoning and 3D intuition, understanding specialized tool use, and displaying planning expertise over a limited number of oracle calls. We benchmark 7 frontier open and closed source LLMs and find even the most performant LLM, GPT5.4, solves only 40.2% of tasks. We hope SMDD-Bench provides a standardized testbed to invigorate the field towards training and evaluating LLM agents for fully autonomous computational drug design. We host a public leaderboard at smddbench.com .

## 1 Introduction 

LLMs have seen considerable success in many scientific discovery tasks such as synthesizing scientific literature, orchestrating chemistry experiments, and even performing end to end applied machine learning research [Boiko et al., 2023, Lu et al., 2026, Asai et al., 2026]. Within the drug discovery and medicinal chemistry space, LLMs have been benchmarked on tasks including single step retrosynthesis prediction, molecular description, de novo molecular generation, and property prediction [Yu et al., 2024, Mirza et al., 2025, Huang et al., 2021, Bartmann et al., 2026]. However, these benchmarks are either restricted to single-turn question answering, are rudimentary and unrealistic when compared to the complexity of real-world computational drug discovery, or are limited to only a small non-diverse handful of tasks and protein targets. In this work, we present SMDD-Bench, an agentic benchmark designed to evaluate LLM agents, multi-agent systems, and agentic workflows on complex and realistic small molecule drug design (SMDD) tasks facing human medicinal chemists. SMDD-Bench is comprised of 5 task types with a total of 502 task instances. The task types are 2D Pharmacophore Identification, Interaction Point Discovery, Scaffold Hopping, Lead Optimization, and Fragment Assembly. Each task is deeply embedded into real-world drug discovery workflows and requires deep chemical intuition, biological reasoning ability, expertise in selecting and running specialized cheminformatics tools, hypothesis generation and validation, and understanding 3D geometries in 3D space. We also introduce the notion of witness-aware task generation, allowing us to generate a large number of chemically valid and guaranteed-solvable task instances procedurally and without a human in the loop. Real world small molecule drug discovery requires the LLM to be able to write RDKit code, analyze protein-ligand interactions in 3D space, propose targeted molecular edits, understand how those molecular edits affect predicted properties, binding affinities, and 3D conformations, and run specialized cheminformatics tools. These skills go far beyond the simple single-turn question answering tasks that previous benchmarks study and require the development of a new and challenging agentic benchmark consisting of a diverse spread of chemistries and protein targets. However, creating real-world drug discovery tasks can be difficult due to the requirement for there to be at least one known solution to the task. For example, in lead optimization, while it is tempting to create a task instance by sampling a protein-ligand pair before sampling a set of properties to optimize as well as a set of properties to hold constant, there is no guarantee there exists a solution that adheres to the set of provided objectives and constraints. As a result, when creating task instances, in SMDD-Bench, we create a witness-aware task generation pipeline, allowing us to generate difficult, chemically valid, and guaranteed-solvable task instances while simultaneously generating an example solution molecule – all without requiring a human expert in the loop. We evaluate 7 frontier open and closed source LLMs using a minimalist agent scaffold on SMDD-Bench and find that, although LLMs achieve up to 50% on simple lead optimization tasks, more challenging lead optimization tasks as well as other task types have near 0% success rates – highlight-ing significant room for growth for LLM applications as autonomous medicinal chemists. We also introduce SMDD-Bench Diversity , a subset of SMDD-Bench along with a principled framework for studying the diversity of SMDD agents’ outputs. We hope that the release of SMDD-Bench standardizes and invigorates research into training and evaluating LLMs for real-world autonomous drug discovery. 22 SMDD-Bench 

SMDD-Bench consists of 502 instances of 5 task types: 2D Pharmacophore Identification (25 tasks), Interaction Point Discovery (25 tasks), Scaffold Hopping (52 tasks), Lead Optimization (340 tasks), and Fragment Assembly (60 tasks). Each task is generated in a witness-aware fashion, where a witness molecule that solves the task is simultaneously generated along with the starting molecule – ensuring the task instance is guaranteed to be solvable and has at least one solution. Each task instance is also evaluated purely computationally, without a human in the loop, using tools such as RDKit, PLIP, OpenBabel, Boltz2, and ADMET-AI [Passaro et al., 2025, Swanson et al., 2024, Landrum, 2010, Salentin et al., 2015, O’Boyle et al., 2011]. Boltz2 is used for protein-ligand co-folded structure prediction, binding affinity 2, and binding probability prediction while the ADMET-AI model is used for property prediction. An overview of the benchmark can be found in Figure 1. 

2.1 Task Types 

Further details on the generation and evaluation of each task type can be found in Appendix B. Only the Scaffold Hopping , Lead Optimization , and Fragment Assembly tasks require witness-aware 

generation due to the 2D Pharmacophore Identification and Interaction Point Discovery tasks having ground truth solutions by default. 

1. 2D Pharmacophore Identification 

Task: Given a target protein sequence as well as the SMILES strings associated with 10 experimentally active molecules and 10 experimentally inactive molecules from the ChEMBL database [Mendez et al., 2019], create a 2D pharmacophore in the form of a python function, which returns True if an inputted SMILES string matches the pharmacophore and False otherwise. See Appendix B.1 for more details. 

Evaluation: The python function is graded on its recall and specificity performance on a held-out set of hidden, experimentally validated actives and inactives. 

Generation: We filtered ChEMBL activity records for high-confidence, direct-binding assays, split the candidates into actives and inactives as determined by potency, and then pruned with MaxMin diversity picking to ensure the visible set spans chemical space and introduces binding mode diversity. This task assesses the agent’s ability to extract a generalizable, discriminative structural hypothesis from a small set of actives and inactives. In real world applications, the pharmacophore produced by the agent can be used as a first-filter for the screening of additional candidate compounds. 

2. Interaction point discovery 

Task: Given a PDB file of the protein structure as well as the location of the pocket, specify the 3D coordinates of the 3 interaction points within a protein pocket most likely to be conserved across a diverse set of small molecule binders. The agent must output a csv file of 3D coordinates as well as a label for the type of interaction point. The types are chosen as donor, acceptor, aromatic, hydrophobic, cation or anion interaction points. 

Evaluation: The predicted points are assigned to the ground-truth points via the Hungarian algorithm. Each prediction is filtered to a ground truth point whose type is compatible (exact match, or a permitted cross-type match, see Appendix B.2 for more details), then the Hungarian algorithm computes the optimal one-to-one assignment under a distance cutoff. All three predictions must match a distinct conserved ground truth point in order for the task to be considered a success. 

Generation: We determine ground truth points by fetching thousands of co-crystal structures per target from the PDB [Berman et al., 2000], aligning binding sites, extracting per-ligand protein interactions, and run density-based clustering with a conservation threshold. Steric clashes with the agent-provided prototype PDB are filtered out and ambiguous type labels are merged. As a result, it is possible for there to be more than three ground truth points of well-conserved interaction points. The agent only needs to output points that match to three of these ground truth points. This task assesses the agent’s ability to identify valuable hotspots within a protein pocket. In real world applications, these hotspots can then be targeted during fragment-based drug discovery

3. Scaffold hopping 

Task: Given a Boltz2-folded protein and reference ligand complex, propose a new SMILES with a distinct scaffold that reproduces the reference ligand’s binding interactions with the protein pocket. 

Evaluation: The submitted SMILES string must have a low Tanimoto similarity with the reference molecule. The Murcko scaffolds of the submitted and reference molecules must also have a maximum common substructure which is only a portion of the overall heavy atom count of the Murcko scaffold of the reference molecule. Both of these checks ensure that a proper scaffold hop has been performed. In order to check if the submitted molecule adheres to the same binding mode as the reference molecule, Boltz2 is used to co-fold the submitted SMILES string against the target using the same pocket contacts as the reference molecule. Potential stochasticity within the Boltz2 co-folded structure is mitigated by matching binding interactions via an ensemble of 10 Boltz2 co-folded structures. 

Witness-Aware Generation: We first mine experimentally known actives for a specific pocket. Af-terwards, we consider all potential pairs of actives. Every pair which the two ligands satisfy the Tanimoto distance and scaffold substructure constraint is co-folded via Boltz to the shared target. If both members of the pair share the same interaction fingerprint with the protein pocket and have high predicted binding probability, the pair and target are created into a task instance. One molecule within the pair is labeled as the reference while the other molecule is labeled as the witness. The witness molecule ensures the existence of at least one solution and guarantees the solvability of the task instance. This task assesses the agent’s ability to understand bioisosterisms as well as internally reason through binding poses before committing to making limited, computationally heavy oracle calls. In real world applications, scaffold hopping is used for rescuing lead molecules with problematic scaffolds, mitigating off-target binding effects, enabling the extension of SAR exploration beyond a depleted search, and avoiding IP infringement. 

4. Lead optimization 

Task: Given a reference molecule that is Boltz2 co-folded with a target protein, optimize the reference molecule into a new molecule that improves a set of ADMET and binding affinity objectives while holding other properties constant, satisfying drug-likeness constraints, maintaining high binding probability, and maintaining close Tanimoto similarity to the reference molecule (due to this being a lead optimization task). 

Evaluation: The submitted molecule must satisfy the set of specified ADMET and binding affinity objectives. RDKit is used to measure drug-likeness, ADMET properties are calculated using the ADMET-AI library, and Boltz2 is used for binding probability and binding affinity prediction. 

Witness-Aware Generation: For each selected protein target, we build a pool of pocket-compatible molecules that pass drug-likeness filters and a Boltz2 binding probability gate. We consider all pairs of molecules from this pool that are similar via Tanimoto similarity. From these pairs, if there exists a molecule whose ADMET/binding affinity profile dominates the other molecule on some subset of the 8 properties in SMDD-Bench (found in Table 7), the task’s optimization objectives and hold-constant constraints are derived from the measured property differences between the two molecules. The dominating molecule becomes the witness while the dominated molecule becomes reference to provide to the agent to start lead optimization. The task instance is only instantiated if the witness’s profile satisfies every objective and constraint. As a result, the witness solves the task instance by construction and the task becomes guaranteed solvable. More details on this can be found in Appendix B.4. This task assesses the agent’s ability to reason through how certain edits may affect complicated, intertwined relationships between molecular properties. In real world applications, multi-property optimization with optimization objectives and hold-constant constraints define the core loop of any lead optimization campaign. Lead optimization is also where the bulk of time is spent in most drug discovery programs. We envision a setting where hundreds of thousands or millions of high-performing agents are instantiated in parallel with the task of discovering a diverse array of optimized molecules given a single starting reference molecule. 45. Fragment Assembly 

Task: Given either one or two 3D molecule fragments placed in a protein pocket, design a single drug-like molecule that contains each fragment as a substructure, covalently links the fragments when more than one fragment is provided, achieves high binding probability and affinity, and preserves the input fragment poses when the submitted molecule is re-docked. 

Evaluation: The submitted molecule must incorporate each of the provided fragments as distinct substructures as well as pass drug-likeness checks. The submitted molecule is also co-folded with the protein via Boltz2 and must have a high predicted binding probability and binding affinity with respect to the witness molecule. Finally, the fragment substructures within the submitted molecule must be similar to the provided 3D coordinates of the input fragments after pocket alignment is performed. This is to ensure a molecule of the same binding mode as the input fragments was designed. Otherwise, it would be possible for a submitted molecule to contain the input fragments but have the fragments be in nonessential locations for binding. More details can be found in Appendix B.5. 

Witness-Aware Generation: We co-fold known actives via Boltz2 and keep the co-folded structures which pass a binding probability gate. We perform BRICS decomposition [Degen et al., 2008] on the molecule to separate the molecule into its synthesizable fragments. Each input fragment must be large enough to be a meaningful pharmacophore, have at least one direct interaction with the protein pocket, and be in close proximity to the protein itself. Fragments are then sampled from the same molecule to create the task instance. As a result, the co-folded active molecule in which fragments are sampled from becomes the witness molecule by construction – ensuring the guaranteed solvability of the task instance. Task instances with 2 fragments have the additional constraint for fragments to be sufficiently far and not directly linked together. This task assesses the agent’s ability to reason through 3D geometry and fragment-pocket comple-mentarity. In real world applications, fragment assembly is the central step of fragment-based drug discovery campaigns, where weakly-binding fragments identified via crystallographic or biophysical screening must be elaborated or linked into a single high-affinity lead molecule. 

2.2 Benchmark Setup and Variations SMDD-Bench Agent Setup. To evaluate the agent’s innate medicinal chemistry reasoning ability, internet access is strictly prohibited, and agents have no access to academic literature, patent filings, textbooks, or any other source of non-parametric knowledge. We benchmark current frontier LLMs using a minimalist ReAct agent [Yao et al., 2022] with a minimal harness, providing access to an isolated filesystem, a python interpreter with relevant biology and chemistry packages installed, and a limited number of Boltz2 and ADMET-AI oracle calls (the same oracles used to evaluate agent submissions). To minimize memorization, all identifying information such as PDB codes, ligand codes, Uniprot IDs, and target names are obfuscated or removed. More details on the minimalist agent can be found in Appendix C. 

Benchmark Statistics. Across 502 task instances, SMDD-Bench contains 102 unique protein targets and 855 unique small molecules. Figure 2 shows the distribution of task types and protein targets across the benchmark. Figure 3(a) and (b) report, respectively, the frequencies with which each of the 9 properties appears in Lead Optimization tasks and the joint distribution of task complexity. Additional details on benchmark diversity are provided in Appendix A. 

SMDD-Bench Lite. Due to the heavy computational overhead of running an agent on all 502 tasks, we release SMDD-Bench Lite, a performance-representative subset of 100 task instances designed to encourage broader adoption of the benchmark. SMDD-Bench Lite serves as a testbed for iterating on agent development against a difficulty-diverse and representative sample of the full benchmark. Details on how the SMDD-Bench Lite instances are selected can be found in Appendix E.

## 4 Common Failure Modes 

Beyond the issues regarding the novelty rate, diversity rate, and enumeration vs. selection performance gap, we investigated individual traces searching for other common failure modes in the frontier LLMs. 

Absence of Cross-Turn Structure–Activity Relationship (SAR) Synthesis. A recurring failure across models is their inability to generalize SAR rules. When a molecule violates predefined constraints, a budget-aware agent should ask whether a specific structural feature caused this failure and apply this exclusion rule to later candidates. Instead, agents repeatedly test variants of already disqualified structures, effectively wasting budget on confirming the same rule multiple times. This reflects a lack of meta-cognitive reasoning: agents fail to convert individual failures into structural generalizations that could narrow down the search space. We provide an annotated reasoning trace in Appendix I.1. 

Incoherent planning across multiple turns. We notice that a common failure mode involves candidate-generation turns being treated independently rather than as part of an iterative, sequential process. This results in the agent generating new molecules instead of selecting from candidates that 8passed earlier-stage criteria. Furthermore, the same molecule may be proposed multiple times across turns even though it had already been tested in a previous turn and did not meet proper thresholds. Refer to a representative trace in Appendix I.2. 

Code Execution Errors. Agents also exhibit tool-specific coding mistakes. These include malformed calls to structure-prediction tools (e.g., incorrect argument types or missing required fields for Boltz binding predictions, cheminformatics rountines (e.g., MolToSmiles conversion), and interaction-analysis pipelines (e.g., PLIP). 

## 5 Related Work 

SMDD-Bench evaluates LLM agents on challenging, real-world small molecule drug design tasks. Existing chemistry and molecular benchmarks – ChemBench [Mirza et al., 2025], MolecularIQ [Bart-mann et al., 2026], SmolInstruct [Yu et al., 2024], QCBench [Xie et al., 2025], and MuMOInstruct [Dey et al., 2025] – are restricted to single-turn question answering on relatively simple chemistry tasks and do not evaluate complex agentic systems on real-world problems. Prior work on multi-turn iterative molecular optimization [Chennakesavalu et al., 2026, Wang et al., 2024] likewise lacks realistic agentic tool calling, instead querying the LLM repeatedly in a loop over a small number of simple optimization objectives. In contrast, harnessing LLM agents as molecular designers lets them use tools to understand the 3D problem setting, analyze previous failed hypotheses, and run simulations and other cheminformatics tools before committing to oracle calls. Iterative prompting approaches are not faithful representations of the true potential for LLM agents in drug discovery. Beyond single-turn QA, prior LLM agents for drug design and discovery [Ock et al., 2026, Li et al., 2026a,b, Zhang et al., 2024, Wang et al., 2024] either report results on a few hand-selected successes or use ad hoc, non-standardized evaluation setups, often retrofitting datasets built for de novo molecular generation such as ZINC, Therapeutics Data Commons, GuacaMol, PMO, and MOSES [Tingle et al., 2023, Huang et al., 2021, Brown et al., 2019, Gao et al., 2022, Polykovskiy et al., 2020]. Adjacent domains like computational biology have produced agentic benchmarks such as DeepResearch Bench [Du et al., 2025], BioProBench [Liu et al., 2025c], BioLP-Bench [Ivanov, 2024], ABC-Bench [Liu et al., 2025a], Lab-Bench [Laurent et al., 2024], BixBench [Mitchener et al., 2025], and CompBioBench [Nair et al., 2026], but no prior work evaluates LLMs on agentic small molecule drug design. The primary contributions of SMDD-Bench compared to previous work are as follows: (1) the first standardized benchmark for evaluating long-horizon, multi-turn, small molecule drug design agents 

on a chemically diverse array of challenging drug discovery tasks; (2) support for five relevant, real-world task types: 2D Pharmacophore Identification, Interaction Point Discovery, Scaffold Hopping, Lead Optimization, and Fragment Assembly; and (3) guaranteed solvability for all tasks, including the Scaffold Hopping, Lead Optimization, and Fragment Assembly tasks via the existence of a held-out witness molecule which passes all evaluation requirements. 

## 6 Discussion and Conclusion 

We present SMDD-Bench, the first agentic benchmark evaluating LLM agents on challenging, long-horizon, real-world small molecule drug design. SMDD-Bench tests scientific and chemical reasoning across diverse chemistries, task types, and protein targets, alongside domain-specific tool-calling capabilities, with every task instance generated to be guaranteed-solvable. Our hope is that SMDD-Bench unifies and standardizes the design, training, and evaluation of LLMs for small molecule drug design. The dominant approach to small molecule drug design today is conditional generative diffusion and flow models [Sun et al., 2025, Zeng et al., 2026, Zhang et al., 2025, Morehead et al., 2026, Klein et al., 2023, Hoogeboom et al., 2022], but LLM-based SMDD agents offer distinct advantages in flexibility and generality. An agent can perform lead optimization on properties unseen at training time, understand the failure modes of specific oracles, selecting the right tool for a given task, and adhere to constraints (such as maintaining protein-protein contacts when designing a PROTAC) that were never specified during training. Communicating objectives in natural language further frees the agent from the rigid train-time settings that bind diffusion and flow models. 

Reliance on Boltz2 and ADMET-AI. Boltz2 and ADMET-AI are imperfect oracles. However, in SMDD-Bench we treat these oracles as the ground truth within the "world" of the benchmark, analogous to an agent requesting wet lab assays. As a result, we deliberately provide a limited number 9of oracle calls to reflect the sparsity of real-world wet lab experiments. To enforce that the Boltz2 and ADMET-AI predicted distributions are the operative ground truth, all PDB-sourced complexes are re-folded via Boltz2 and re-scored by ADMET-AI during task generation, so the agent only sees structure-activity relationships and property distributions produced by these oracles. SMDD-Bench is intended as a testbed for developing training frameworks calibrated to the Boltz2 and ADMET-AI distributions. The same frameworks can later be interfaced with an autonomous laboratory, where the oracle becomes a real wet lab that synthesizes, characterizes, and tests candidates, allowing agents to be re-trained under the true distribution of real-world structure-activity relationships .

## B Details on Task Type Generation 

In the following section, we provide the specific details for generating the task instances for each task type. 

B.1 2D Pharmacophore Identification 

Each instance of this task type asks the agent to identify a 2D pharmacophore that distinguishes a set of active molecules from a set of inactive molecules for a given protein target. Tasks are generated procedurally from ChEMBL. 

Activity data extraction We restrict to direct binding assays by requiring standard_type ∈{IC 50 , K i}, standard_units = nM, standard_relation = ‘=’, and confidence_score ≥ 8.Only targets with target_type = ‘SINGLE PROTEIN’ are considered. 

Compound normalization and deduplication. SMILES are canonicalized via RDKit. We discard multi-component salts/mixtures, molecules with exact molecular weight greater than 900 Da, and entries that fail RDKit parsing. Multiple measurements of the same canonical compound are collapsed by taking the per-compound median activity in nM. 

Active/inactive labeling Compounds with median activity below 1,000 nM (IC 50 /Ki < 1 μM) are labeled active; compounds with median activity above 10,000 nM ( > 10 μM) are labeled inactive. Compounds in the 1–10 μM ambiguous zone are discarded, providing a 10 × potency gap between the two classes that excludes borderline molecules. Targets with fewer than 30 actives or fewer than 30 inactives after filtering are not eligible. 

Diverse subset selection For each class, ten visible exemplars are selected via the MaxMin algorithm operating on Morgan fingerprints (radius = 2 , 2048 bits, seed = 42 ) [Ashton et al., 2002]. The remaining compounds form a held-out evaluation set that the agent never sees. MaxMin selection forces the visible exemplars to span chemical space, allowing the agent to see the most chemically diverse spread of actives as possible. 

Agent inputs and outputs Each generated task directory contains: actives.smi 

and inactives.smi (the 10 visible exemplars per class, given to the agent), 

protein.fasta (the target sequence). The agent is required to emit solution.py defin-ing check_pharmacophore(smiles: str) -> bool , which is run on every molecule of the held-out evaluation set during scoring. 

Design rationale Using ChEMBL-measured inactives rather than synthetically constructed decoys (e.g. DUD-E) ensures that negatives are experimentally verified non-binders that may share scaffolds with the actives, ruling out solutions that rely on coarse topological dissimilarity. Providing both classes to the agent and requiring generalization to a held-out set forces extraction of a genuine substructural pattern rather than memorization of the visible exemplars. 15 0 5 10 15 20 25 30

B.2 Interaction Point Discovery 

Each instance of this task type asks the agent to predict, given a single apo-like protein structure and a pocket center, the 3D coordinates and pharmacophoric type of the three interaction points most likely to be conserved across diverse binders of that pocket. Tasks are generated procedurally from the RCSB PDB [Berman et al., 2000]. 

Ensemble construction For each specified target from the PDB, we fetch up to 500 unique X-ray crystal structures with resolution better than 2.5 Å. The first structure containing a drug-like ligand is taken as the reference ; common crystallization additives (glycerol, PEG, DMSO, sulfate, and similar) are explicitly blacklisted. Within that reference structure, the largest non-blacklisted ligand by heavy-atom count is selected as the reference ligand. The reference receptor is stripped of all heteroatoms, waters, and ions to produce receptor.pdb , and the centroid of the reference ligand defines the pocket center provided to the agent. 

Local structural alignment All ensemble members are superimposed onto the reference using only the C α atoms of residues within 12 Å of the pocket center. Restricting the alignment to the local pocket suppresses contributions from global protein motion and induced fit – minimizing potential global variance in the 3D reference frame. 

Pharmacophore feature extraction For each aligned structure, we select the ligand whose centroid lies closest to the reference pocket center (within 12 Å); waters, the same blacklist of crystallization additives and common ions used during reference selection, and small fragments (fewer than six heavy atoms) are excluded from candidacy. RDKit is then used to assign each atom or functional group of the chosen ligand to one of six pharmacophoric families: hydrogen-bond donor, hydrogen-bond acceptor, aromatic, hydrophobic, cation, or anion. Features further than 12 Å from the pocket center are discarded to exclude allosteric binders and crystallographic artifacts. 

Consensus clustering Clustering proceeds in two stages. First, DBSCAN [Ester et al., 1996] is run independently per pharmacophore family at ε = 1 .0 Å on the 3D points of that family alone, so that, e.g., donor and acceptor densities never collapse into a single cluster regardless of spatial proximity. A cluster is retained only if it is supported by at least 15% of the unique structures contributing to the ensemble; the conservation score of a cluster is the fraction of unique PDB entries supporting it. Cross-family relationships between surviving clusters are handled separately during ground-truth point selection. 

Ground-truth point selection Surviving clusters are post-processed to yield a physically valid, non-redundant ground-truth set: (i) clusters within 2 Å of any heavy atom of the reference receptor are discarded as sterically clashing with the reference protein conformation given to the agent; (ii) clusters of different types (e.g. donor and cation arising from a basic amine, or donor and acceptor from a hydroxyl) whose centroids lie within 2 Å are merged into a single point with a set-valued type label, reflecting and allowing for chemical ambiguity in the agent output. The full set of N ≥ 3

surviving consensus points constitutes the ground truth. 18 Agent inputs and outputs The agent is provided receptor.pdb (the reference protein with all ligands and solvent removed) the pocket center coordinates and the 12 Å pocket radius where all interaction points are within. The agent must emit solution.csv with exactly three rows of the form x, y, z, type , where type is drawn from { Donor , Acceptor , Aromatic , Hydrophobic ,

Cation , Anion }. 

Evaluation Predicted points are matched against the N ground-truth points by optimal bipartite assignment [Kuhn, 1955] on Euclidean distance, restricted to type-compatible pairs: a prediction is type-compatible with a ground-truth point if its label is in the ground-truth label set, with an additional aromatic/hydrophobic equivalence at scoring time, reflecting that aromatic ring features and hydrophobic features are partially co-tagged by RDKit and that the ground-truth construction may assign only one of the two labels at a position where both are chemically reasonable. This choice was made out of evaluation leniency. A match is counted if the assigned distance is below 2.5 Å. The task score is (number of matched predictions) / 3, and the task is considered solved only when all three predictions match (score = 1 .0). 

Design rationale The local-pocket alignment, conservation threshold, and steric clash filter were designed to ensure that the targets are simultaneously experimentally grounded, chemotype-agnostic, empirically solvable, and medicinally useful in the specific receptor conformation handed to the agent. 

B.3 Scaffold Hopping 

Each instance of this task type asks the agent to propose a novel small molecule that binds the same protein pocket as a given reference ligand and reproduces its protein–ligand interaction pattern, while presenting a chemically distinct scaffold. Tasks are generated procedurally from the RCSB PDB and ChEMBL, and every task is constructed around an explicit witness molecule that is known and evaluated a priori to satisfy the evaluation criteria – thus guaranteeing the solvability of the task instance. 

Target and canonical structure selection Targets are sourced from a curated pool of ∼300 

(UniProt, PDB, ligand) triplets spanning diverse protein families, grouped by UniProt identifier. For each UniProt, the PDB entry with the best X-ray resolution is chosen as the canonical receptor; its FASTA sequence and a single fixed pocket definition are reused throughout downstream steps. 

Pocket definition The binding pocket is defined as the set of residues with any heavy atom within 6 Å of the canonical ligand. This pocket, expressed as (chain, SEQRES position) pairs, is held fixed for all downstream Boltz2 cofolding runs and is also handed to the agent as part of the task specification. 

Candidate ligand collection For each target we assemble a candidate ligand pool from two sources: (i) all ligands co-crystallized with the same UniProt across the PDB, with bond orders corrected via Chemical Component Dictionary (CCD) templates and deduplicated by canonical SMILES; and (ii) ChEMBL actives against the target with reported IC 50 /Ki/Kd below 10 μM, pre-filtered to remove unparseable structures, molecules above 1,000 Da, and metal-containing species. 

Scaffold-diversity pre-filter Bemis-Murcko scaffolds are extracted for every candidate, and for each unordered ligand pair we compute the Maximum Common Substructure (MCS) of the two scaffolds [Bemis and Murcko, 1996]. A pair is retained only if the MCS covers less than 65% of the heavy atoms of the larger scaffold; the symmetric denominator ensures the filter does not depend on the eventual reference/witness role assignment. 

Crystal-PLIP pre-ranking For pairs in which both members appear as PDB co-crystals, each source structure is superimposed onto the canonical receptor using C α atoms of pocket residues within 12 Å, and a protein-ligand interaction fingerprint is extracted with PLIP [Salentin et al., 2015]. Pairs are then ordered by descending crystal interaction-fingerprint similarity, with PDB-PDB pairs prioritized over pairs involving ChEMBL-only ligands. This ranking concentrates downstream compute on pairs that are most likely to clear the interaction-similarity gate during the co-folding screen. 

Co-folding screen For each target, the top-ranked unique candidate ligands are co-folded with the canonical receptor using Boltz2 [Passaro et al., 2025], constrained to the fixed pocket definition with 10 diffusion samples per ligand. From the resulting ensemble of 10 conformations, we extract: (i) the Boltz2-predicted binding probability across samples; (ii) per-sample PLIP interaction finger-19 prints, where each interaction is coarsened to a (chain, residue, broad type) tuple over six interaction families (HBond, Aromatic, Ionic, Halogen, Metal, Hydrophobic); and (iii) a consensus interaction fingerprint defined as the set of tuples present in at least 60% of the diffusion samples. Any ligand whose predicted binding probability falls below 0.75 is discarded. The consensus interaction fingerprint is required during evaluation to offset variance induced by Boltz2-predicted binding poses. 

Witness validation and role assignment A pair is promoted to a task only if both members survive the binding gate and the set Tanimoto between their consensus interaction fingerprints exceeds 0.75. Within a surviving pair, the ligand with the higher predicted binding probability is assigned as the 

reference (the molecule provided to the agent to hop away from) and the other becomes the witness (the hidden molecule whose existence guarantees the solvability of the task instance). We retain at most three tasks per UniProt target and require distinct reference molecules across tasks of the same target, preventing one well-behaved reference from dominating the benchmark. 

Agent inputs and outputs The agent is given: complex.pdb (the Boltz2-predicted reference complex, with the ligand on chain L), reference.sdf (the reference ligand conformer extracted from the Boltz2 complex), protein.fasta (the canonical protein sequence), and the target ligand identifier and pocket contact residue list used during the task generation process. The agent is required to emit solution.smi containing a single SMILES string. The witness molecule is never exposed to the agent. 

Evaluation A submission must clear five gates: (1) the SMILES is parseable by RDKit; (2) the Morgan fingerprint Tanimoto (radius = 2 , 2048 bits) between the agent’s molecule and the reference is below 0.5; (3) the Bemis–Murcko scaffold MCS, normalized by the heavy-atom count of the larger of the two scaffolds, is below 0.65; (4) the agent’s molecule, co-folded under the same Boltz protocol used during generation (10 diffusion samples, identical pocket constraint), produces a consensus interaction fingerprint whose set Tanimoto with the reference’s consensus fingerprint exceeds 0.75; and (5) the predicted binding probability of the agent’s molecule across the same diffusion samples exceeds 0.7. Gates 1, 2, and 3 enforce 2D novelty; gates 4 and 5 enforce 3D mimicry, ensuring the scaffold hopped molecule adheres to the same pharmacophore, and binding competence. 

Design rationale Every task is anchored to a concrete witness molecule that has already cleared the same evaluation pipeline the agent will be scored against, ensuring the existence of at least one solution for the task instance. The 2D-dissimilarity and 3D-interaction-similarity criteria rule evaluates for a true scaffold hopped molecule and that the submitted molecule matches the binding mode of the original reference molecule. High predicted binding probability of the submitted molecule is also enforced. We score interaction similarity against a consensus fingerprint over the 10-sample diffusion ensemble rather than against the single most confident pose to ensure robustness against variance in the Boltz2 poses. Requiring a tuple to appear in at least 60% of samples discards interactions that are artifacts of any one diffusion draw and retains only those the model is confident enough to recover under structural perturbation. 

B.4 Lead Optimization 

Each instance of this task type asks the agent to modify a known active molecule so that it improves a specified set of pharmacological optimization objectives, holds a separate set of properties within tolerance, and clears a fixed panel of hard drug-likeness constraints. As in the Scaffold Hopping task type, every task instance is constructed around an explicit witness molecule that has already been verified to satisfy every gate the agent will be scored against, guaranteeing the solvability of the task instance. 

Target and pocket selection Targets are drawn from a curated set of UniProt entries with high-resolution co-crystal coverage. For each target, a single representative PDB is chosen; its FASTA sequence and the residues within 6 Å of the crystal ligand define a fixed pocket that is reused for all candidate co-folding within the target. 

Candidate pool collection For each target we aggregate experimentally reported actives from ChEMBL, BindingDB, and PubChem, deduplicated by canonical SMILES. The pool is then filtered through the same hard-constraint panel that will be applied to the agent’s submission at evaluation time. Design rationale Deriving objectives and hold-constant sets from the actual reference–witness property delta, rather than imposing a fixed objective list per target, produces tasks that provably solvable. The high-Tanimoto pairing constraint enforces that the task captures local lead-series chemistry rather than de novo design, which is the regime in which the multi-objective trade-offs are most relevant. Because the entire hard-constraint panel and the ADMET model are applied identically at generation and at evaluation, the agent is scored under the exact filter that defined task feasibility, and any submission that clears the gates is at least as good as a known real molecule on every property the task names. We investigate the indicators of the difficulty of the Lead Optimization tasks in Section D.1. 

B.5 Fragment Assembly 

Each instance of this task type asks the agent to design a single drug-like molecule that incorporates one or two given 3D molecular fragments at their specified poses inside a protein binding pocket, covalently links them when more than one is provided, and binds the target well. As in the previous two task types, every task instance is constructed around an explicit witness molecule – here, the known active from which the fragments were carved – that has already been verified to satisfy every gate the agent will be scored against, guaranteeing the solvability of the task instance. 

Target and active selection Targets are sourced from UniProt entries that have at least two PDB entries containing distinct, non-buffer ligands of at least 10 heavy atoms, after filtering crystallization additives. For each (UniProt, PDB, ligand) triplet, the ligand SMILES is reconstructed via Chemical Component Dictionary templates and the binding pocket is defined as the protein residues with any heavy atom within 6 Å of the ligand. 

Witness co-folding Each candidate active is co-folded against its source protein with Boltz2 [Passaro et al., 2025] under pocket constraints derived from the crystal pose, with the affinity head enabled and 10 diffusion samples. Only chains that participate in pocket contacts are retained for the co-fold; co-ligands and unrelated chains are excluded. Actives are kept only if their predicted binding probability exceeds 0.75. The resulting predicted log 10 (IC 50 ) is recorded as the witness affinity baseline against which the agent’s submission will be scored. 

Fragment generation Surviving actives are decomposed via BRICS [Degen et al., 2008] into chemically reasonable fragments at retrosynthetically meaningful bonds. Each fragment is retained only if it (i) contains at least 6 heavy atoms after dummy-atom removal, (ii) makes at least one PLIP [Salentin et al., 2015] protein-ligand interaction with the receptor when scored on the witness’s docked pose, and (iii) lies within 4 Å of the protein. The first criterion ensures the fragment is large enough to be a meaningful pharmacophore rather than a methyl-scale stub; the second and third together guarantee that the fragment is genuinely engaged with the pocket rather than solvent-exposed. 

Pair sampling for two-fragment tasks When a task instance is to contain two fragments, the pair is drawn from a single source active and is required to have a closest-heavy-atom distance of at least 6 Å, this prevents the fragments from being spatially close enough to admit a short or simple linker. 

Agent inputs and outputs The agent is given: pocket.pdb (the witness’s Boltz2-predicted protein structure with the witness ligand stripped), one fragment_i.sdf per fragment (the 3D pose of each fragment as it sat in the witness’s Boltz2 docked complex), the binding affinity baseline target for the submited molecule, and the full hard-constraint panel also used in the Lead Optimization task (see Table 6). The agent is required to emit solution.smi containing a single SMILES string. The witness is never exposed to the agent. 22 Evaluation A submission must clear five gates: (1) the SMILES is valid; (2) every fragment SMARTS pattern is a substructure of the agent’s molecule; (3) the fragments admit a disjoint substructure assignment (no shared atoms across fragments) and are pairwise connected by a covalent path in the agent’s molecule; (4) the agent’s molecule clears the same hard-constraint panel specified in Table 6; and (5) Boltz2 co-folding of the agent’s molecule against the source receptor under the same pocket constraint and 10 diffusion samples produces (a) a predicted binding probability above 0.7, (b) a predicted log 10 (IC 50 ) no more than 0.1 log units worse than the witness baseline, and (c) a per-fragment 3D pose RMSD below 2.0 Å. RMSD is computed by SMARTS-matching each fragment against the docked agent ligand, extracting the 3D coordinates of the matched atoms, aligning the docked complex to pocket.pdb by Kabsch superposition on the C α atoms of the pocket residues, and selecting the best-aligned diffusion sample for the comparison. 

Design rationale The single-active witness construction guarantees that for every task there exists at least one molecule – the original active itself – that contains all fragments at exactly the required poses, links them through known chemistry, and already satisfies the binding and drug-likeness panels under the same Boltz2 protocol used at evaluation time. The 3D pose RMSD threshold enforces the docked geometry to recapitulate the fragment poses given as input, which forces the linker chemistry to be physically compatible with the relative orientation of the fragments rather than just being topologically valid or bind well.

23 C.1 Selecting Oracle Calls 

By default, across all evaluations, we set the total number of available Boltz2 calls to 8 and the number of ADMET-AI calls to 15. Note that a single ADMET-AI call returns all 8 ADMET properties for the inputted SMILES string. The complete reasoning behind only providing the LLM with a limited number of oracle calls is outlined in Section 6. In short, our goal is to treat the oracle calls as though they were ground truth, real-world wet lab experiments, hence the limited number of oracle calls. Ultimately, SMDD-Bench can be considered a testbed for developing the right training strategies and agent harnesses for SMDD agents – even though agents trained on SMDD-Bench adhere to the Boltz2/ADMET-AI distribution of binding affinities, structures, and properties. Afterwards, the agents can be trained using those same strategies with a wet-lab-in-the-loop – where the oracle calls are directly made to an automated wet lab, performing the synthesis and then the pertinent assays completely autonomously. The amount of Boltz2 and ADMET-AI calls must be tight enough to reward oracle-efficient rea-soning instead of brute-force enumeration but generous enough such that a competent agent is not bottlenecked by oracle access. In order to determine a standard number of oracle calls, we performed a sweep across oracle budget choices using a small calibration subset. We constructed the calibration split of 25 tasks drawn proportionally from each task type, with a minimum of one task per type (each task yielding 

max(1 , ⌊25 · nt/N ⌋), where nt is the number of tasks of type t and N the total benchmark size). The Lead Optimization task instances were sampled stratified by problem complexity, defined as the sum of the number of optimization objectives and the number of hold-constant constraint, such that the calibration set spans the full difficulty range of the task type. We swept each oracle independently while pinning the other to a generous ceiling: • Boltz sweep: ADMET budget fixed at 35 ; Boltz budget varied over {5, 8, 12 }.• ADMET sweep: Boltz budget fixed at 12 ; ADMET budget varied over {15 , 25 , 35 }.The shared end-point (boltz = 12 , admet = 35) was reused, giving five distinct (B, A ) configurations in total. All other agent-harness settings were held at their defaults; the agent model used for calibration was GPT5.4 .For each sweep, we computed the per-type pass rate at every budget level and selected the "knee" of the plot, where the increase in performance began to saturate as the number of oracle calls increased.

D.2 Analysis on inter-agent submission diversity

For each task we define a stratum used as a difficul-ty/discrimination proxy: • Lead optimization : the tuple (nopt , n hold ) of the number of optimisation objectives and hold-constant properties. • 2D Pharmacophore ID, Interaction Point Discovery, Scaffold Hopping, Fragment Assembly : the integer success rate of the task across the seven evaluated models, 

Stratum allocation. Given a per-type budget k and stratum sizes {ns}, we allocate k slots across strata in proportion to ns using the largest-remainder (Hamilton) rule: each stratum gets ⌊k · ns/N ⌋

slots, and the remaining slots are awarded to the strata with the largest fractional parts, breaking ties by stratum key.

## F Selecting SMDD-Bench Diversity 

During a lead optimization campaign, one potential use case involves running many SMDD agents on the same task in parallel in order to achieve a diverse spread of successful molecules. In order to test this, we develop the SMDD-Bench Diversity subset, a standardized 20 instance subset of SMDD-Bench meant for each task instance to be run multiple times. In order to select the SMDD-Bench Diversity subset, we seek to find the most challenging consistently solvable instances. Task instances that are too simple are trivially solvable with minimal molecular edits. Task instances which are too challenging may be out of reach for the current frontier LLMs. We first rank all Lead Optimization task instances solvable by 5/7 LLMs and rank them by turn count. We use turn count as a proxy for ranking the difficulty and chemical solution space of a task instance, following insight from Appendix D.2. We then hand-select the 20 instances with the highest average turn counts across the 7 LLMs while ensuring target diversity (no single target dominates the subset). This selection strategy ensures the tasks are at the edge of the realm of solvable tasks for current frontier LLM agents. 29 Table 15: The task breakdown for SMDD-Bench Lite compared to SMDD-Bench.

## G Enumeration vs. Selection Details 

A failure on SMDD-Bench can come from two qualitatively different shortcomings of the agent: (i) the agent never enumerated a passing molecule at all, or (ii) the agent enumerated a passing molecule somewhere in its trajectory but never selected it to be tested by an oracle call. The enumeration vs. selection experiments are designed to disentangle these shortcomings: for every failed task, we extract and evaluate every SMILES string enumerated by the agent but not selected for an oracle call. We define a “recovered” task to be one whose original submission fails evaluation but where at least one trajectory-resident SMILES would have passed the full evaluator. We run the experiment for all seven evaluated models and the three generation-style task types: Scaffold Hopping, Lead Optimization, and Fragment Assembly. 2D Pharmacophore Identification and Interaction Point Discovery are excluded because their outputs are not molecules. In order to extract the SMILES string, we compact each trajectory to only the reasoning trace, LLM output, and python interpreter input before feeding the compacted trajectory to GPT5.4-mini-high.
